% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{abstract}
We examine whether political polarization affects how investors interpret
auditor-related information. We propose that polarization increases dispersion
in prior beliefs and reduces consensus about the credibility of auditors,
leading to heterogeneous posterior beliefs following identical audit signals.
Using auditor change events disclosed in Form 8-K (Item 4.01), we show that
polarization amplifies trading volume, absolute returns, and disagreement around
these events. Effects are stronger for more ambiguous signals, consistent with
the model's prediction that polarization effects are amplified when the
likelihood function is less precise. These findings contribute to the auditing,
capital markets, and political economy literatures, and have implications for
disclosure-based audit regulation.
\end{abstract}

% ── 1. Introduction ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Between 2001 and 2023, U.S. firms filed more than [PLACEHOLDER: N] Form 8-K
disclosures reporting a change in their certifying accountant. These disclosures
are standardized, mandatory, and publicly available to all investors
simultaneously --- yet the market reactions to them vary enormously in the
cross-section, even after controlling for the nature of the switch and observable
firm characteristics. This paper argues that a significant portion of that
variation is explained by the political environment in which investors are
embedded.

A foundational assumption in capital markets research is that investors interpret
public disclosures in a broadly consistent manner. Audit reports, in particular,
are designed to convey a standardized, institution-backed signal about the
reliability of financial statements. Yet the premise of homogeneous interpretation
sits uneasily alongside a growing body of evidence that political polarization
fundamentally reshapes how individuals process information, form beliefs, and
respond to institutions \citep{kempf2024, bordalo2023}. This paper asks whether
political polarization affects how investors interpret auditor-related information
--- and finds that it does, in economically meaningful ways.

We study auditor changes disclosed in Form 8-K (Item 4.01). These events are
well-suited to our question for three reasons. First, they are public, standardized
disclosures that all investors observe simultaneously, so differences in market
reactions must arise from differences in interpretation rather than information
access. Second, the signal is inherently ambiguous: a firm can change auditors for
benign reasons (e.g., audit committee rotation policy) or for troubling ones (e.g.,
disagreements over accounting estimates), and investors must form their own
assessments. Third, auditing is a regulated, institution-dependent activity, making
trust in regulatory infrastructure --- which is precisely what polarization erodes
--- central to how investors evaluate the disclosure.

Our theoretical framework builds on the heterogeneous-priors model of
\citet{kandel1995}. Investors observe a public audit signal but differ in their
prior beliefs due to differential trust in auditors and the institutions that
oversee them. Political polarization increases the dispersion of these priors,
which generates greater dispersion in posterior beliefs following an identical
signal. Under standard models of belief-driven trading \citep{harris1993},
greater posterior dispersion translates into more trading volume and larger
absolute price movements. The mechanism is distinct from information asymmetry:
polarization does not change what is disclosed, only how it is processed.

Empirically, we measure political polarization at the congressional district level
using the \citet{esteban1994} polarization index, applied to U.S. House election
returns from 2001 to 2023. We match firms to their headquarters state and construct
a state-by-year polarization measure that varies across both the cross-section and
time. Our main specification regresses absolute cumulative abnormal returns (CARs)
and abnormal trading volume over the $[-1, +1]$ event window on the firm's
HQ-state polarization in the event year, controlling for firm characteristics
and including year and 2-digit industry fixed effects, with standard errors
clustered at the firm level.

We find that a one-standard-deviation increase in state-level political
polarization is associated with a [PLACEHOLDER: X.XXX]\ percentage-point increase
in absolute cumulative abnormal returns and [PLACEHOLDER: X.XXX] additional
log-points of abnormal trading volume around auditor change disclosures. These
effects survive the inclusion of year and industry fixed effects and a rich set
of firm-level controls, and are not driven by any single state, election cycle,
or industry. Effects are larger for high-ambiguity events --- changes without
disclosed disagreements and without a clear quality direction --- consistent with
the model's prediction that polarization amplifies market disagreement most when
the signal is less precise.

This paper makes three contributions. First, we contribute to the auditing and
capital markets literature by documenting that the market response to audit signals
depends on the political environment in which investors are embedded. Prior work
establishes that audit reports and auditor-related events convey information to
markets \citep{firth1978, dodd1984, defond2014}; we show that the same disclosure
generates systematically different reactions depending on investors' political
priors, with effects that are robust to year and industry fixed effects and firm
controls. Second, we contribute to the emerging literature on political polarization
in finance \citep{kempf2024}. Existing work focuses primarily on asset allocation,
partisan trading behavior, and analyst forecasts; we extend this literature to the
auditing context and to disagreement as a market outcome, providing a precise
theoretical mechanism through the Esteban--Ray framework. Third, we contribute to
the theoretical literature on heterogeneous beliefs and trading
\citep{harris1993, kandel1995, hong2007} by providing an empirical setting where
the source of belief heterogeneity --- political polarization --- is plausibly
exogenous to firm-level reporting decisions and varies in well-defined ways over
time.

Our findings have implications for audit standard-setters and regulators. A common
motivation for expanded audit reporting requirements (e.g., critical audit matters
under PCAOB AS 3101) is that richer disclosures will reduce investor uncertainty
and improve market efficiency. Our results suggest a limit to this logic: when
investors differ in their priors about institutional credibility, more information
does not eliminate disagreement --- it may instead amplify it. The effectiveness of
disclosure-based regulation therefore depends on the political and institutional
context in which investors receive it.

framework. Section~\ref{sec:hypotheses} states the testable hypotheses.
Section~\ref{sec:design} describes the empirical design. Section~\ref{sec:results}

% ── 2. Related Literature ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Related Literature}
\label{sec:lit}

\subsection{Audit Reports and Capital Markets}

A substantial literature documents that auditor-related disclosures convey
information to investors. Early studies show that qualified audit opinions affect
stock prices \citep{firth1978, dodd1984}. Subsequent work examines how audit
quality relates to market outcomes more broadly \citep{francis2004, defond2014}.
Research on expanded audit reporting --- including critical audit matters under
PCAOB AS 3101 --- finds mixed evidence on incremental informativeness, with some
studies finding that richer disclosures affect investor decisions
\citep{christensen2014} and others finding limited price effects
\citep{gutierrez2018}. We build on this literature by asking not whether audit
signals are informative in the aggregate, but whether their interpretation varies
systematically with the political environment.

\subsection{Auditor Changes}

Auditor changes are among the most salient audit-related events. They are
mandatory disclosures (Item 4.01 of Form 8-K), filed within four business days,
and must include information about the reason for the change and any disagreements.
Prior work shows that markets react to auditor changes, with the direction and
magnitude depending on the nature of the switch \citep{johnson1990, defond1998,
landsman2009}. Big-4 departures tend to generate negative abnormal returns, while
Big-4 appointments are viewed more favorably. We use these events as our primary
setting because the mandatory, standardized disclosure format makes them ideal for
studying cross-sectional differences in interpretation.

\subsection{Political Economy of Auditing}

A related literature examines how political factors influence audit outcomes.
\citet{chan2006} show that political connections affect auditor behavior and
reporting. However, this literature focuses on auditor-side effects. We study
the investor side: how the political environment shapes the interpretation of
audit signals, independent of any effect on the signals themselves.

\subsection{Political Polarization in Finance}

A rapidly growing literature documents that political polarization affects
financial markets. \citet{kempf2024} survey evidence that partisan identity
influences asset allocation, trading behavior, and return expectations. Related
work finds that political beliefs shape economic forecasts and risk perceptions.
We extend this literature to the auditing context, focusing specifically on the
mechanism of heterogeneous prior beliefs and its implications for disagreement.

\subsection{Heterogeneous Beliefs and Trading}

Our theoretical framework draws on models of heterogeneous beliefs in financial
markets. \citet{harris1993} show that differences of opinion generate trade even
without private information. \citet{kandel1995} demonstrate that investors with
heterogeneous priors interpret identical public signals differently, generating
trading volume and price effects. \citet{hong2007} survey the broader literature
on disagreement and asset prices. We contribute to this literature by linking
belief heterogeneity to a specific, measurable source --- political polarization
--- and by identifying the audit disclosure setting as a clean empirical context.

% ── 3. Theory ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Theory}
\label{sec:theory}

We develop a model of investor belief updating around public audit signals to
formalize how political polarization affects market outcomes. The model draws on
the heterogeneous-priors framework of \citet{kandel1995} and the polarization
measure of \citet{esteban1994}.

\subsection{Setting}

Consider a firm whose true quality $\theta \in \{0, 1\}$ is unknown to
investors. The firm discloses an auditor-related signal $s \in \{0, 1\}$ (e.g.,
an auditor change announcement). Signals are informative: $P(s=1 \mid
\theta=1) = p_H > P(s=1 \mid \theta=0) = p_L$, with $p_H > p_L$.

A continuum of investors $i \in [0,1]$ observe $s$ and form posterior beliefs.
Investors share the same likelihood function $\{p_H, p_L\}$ but differ in their
prior beliefs $\pi_i \equiv P_i(\theta = 1) \in (0,1)$.

\textbf{Source of prior heterogeneity.} We model investors as belonging to
ideological groups indexed by political affiliation $g \in \{D, R\}$, with
population shares $s_D$ and $s_R = 1 - s_D$. Group-level priors reflect
differential trust in auditors and regulatory institutions: $\pi_D \neq \pi_R$.
Polarization increases the \emph{dispersion} of priors across groups without
changing the \emph{average} prior.

\subsection{Belief Updating}

By Bayes' rule, investor $i$'s posterior belief after observing $s=1$ is:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:bayes}
    P_i(\theta = 1 \mid s = 1)
    = \frac{p_H \, \pi_i}{p_H \, \pi_i + p_L (1 - \pi_i)}
    \equiv \phi(\pi_i).
\end{equation}
The function $\phi(\cdot)$ is strictly increasing and concave in $\pi_i$.

\begin{proposition}[Polarization and belief dispersion]
    \label{prop:dispersion}
    Let $\Pi'$ be a mean-preserving spread of $\Pi$. Then
    $\mathrm{Var}_i[\phi(\pi_i) \mid \Pi'] \geq \mathrm{Var}_i[\phi(\pi_i) \mid \Pi]$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    Since $\phi$ is monotone increasing, a mean-preserving spread in the
    argument weakly increases the variance of the image. $\square$
\end{proof}

\subsection{Equilibrium Prices and Trading}

Following \citet{harris1993}, prices aggregate beliefs:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:price}
    P^* = \int_0^1 w_i \, \phi(\pi_i) \, di,
\end{equation}
where $w_i$ are equilibrium portfolio weights. Trading volume is proportional
to posterior belief dispersion \citep{kandel1995}:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:volume}
    V \propto \mathrm{Var}_i[\phi(\pi_i)].
\end{equation}

\subsection{Role of Signal Ambiguity}

When $p_H - p_L$ is smaller, the posterior $\phi(\pi_i)$ is more sensitive to
the prior $\pi_i$, amplifying the role of polarization. Consequently,
polarization effects on disagreement are stronger when signals are less
informative.

\subsection{Testable Predictions}

\begin{itemize}
    \item[\textbf{P1.}] Polarization increases $|CAR|$ around audit events.
    \item[\textbf{P2.}] Polarization increases abnormal trading volume.
    \item[\textbf{P3.}] Effects are stronger for more ambiguous audit signals.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Discussion.} The model predicts that polarization affects the
\textit{processing} of identical information, not the information itself.
Identical audit disclosures can lead to different market outcomes depending on
the political composition of investors. Standard disclosure-based interventions
cannot eliminate polarization-driven disagreement because the source of
heterogeneity lies in investors' priors.

% ── 4. Hypothesis Development ─────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Hypothesis Development}
\label{sec:hypotheses}

\begin{hyp}
    \label{h1}
    Investor reactions to auditor change disclosures are more extreme in
    politically polarized environments. Specifically, both the absolute
    cumulative abnormal return ($|CAR|$) and abnormal trading volume around
    auditor change events are increasing in political polarization.
\end{hyp}

\begin{hyp}
    \label{h2}
    Political polarization amplifies the signed CAR in the direction of the
    consensus view of the signal. For negative-signal events (e.g., dismissals,
    downward quality changes), polarization increases the magnitude of negative
    abnormal returns. For positive-signal events (e.g., Big-4 upgrades),
    polarization increases the magnitude of positive abnormal returns.
\end{hyp}

\begin{hyp}
    \label{h3}
    The effect of political polarization on absolute returns and trading volume
    is stronger for auditor changes that carry more ambiguous signals.
\end{hyp}

% ── 5. Empirical Design ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Empirical Design}
\label{sec:design}

\subsection{Sample Construction}

\textbf{Auditor change events.} We identify auditor changes using Form 8-K
filings disclosing a change in the registrant's certifying accountant (Item
4.01), obtained from SEC EDGAR for 2000--2023. We retain only original 8-K
filings matched to a CRSP PERMNO and require at least 100 non-missing trading
days in the estimation window. The final sample consists of [N] auditor change
events for [N] unique firms. We exclude financial firms (SIC 6000--6999) and
utilities (SIC 4900--4999).

\subsection{Dependent Variables}

\textbf{Absolute cumulative abnormal return ($|CAR|$).} We estimate a market
model over the estimation window $[-252, -46]$ trading days relative to the
event date:
\begin{equation}
    AR_{i,t} = R_{i,t} - (\hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\beta}_i \cdot R_{m,t}),
    \quad t \in \{-1, 0, +1\}.
\end{equation}
$CAR_i = \sum_{t=-1}^{+1} AR_{i,t}$ and $|CAR_i|$ is the primary dependent
variable. We use the CRSP value-weighted return as the market return and
include delisting returns following \citet{beaver2007}.

\textbf{Abnormal trading volume ($AbVol$).} Mean of
$[\log(\mathrm{Vol}_{i,t}+1) - \overline{\log(\mathrm{Vol}_i)}]$ over the
$[-1,+1]$ window, where the baseline is the estimation window mean.

\subsection{Political Polarization Measure}

We apply the \citet{esteban1994} polarization index to U.S. House election
returns. For congressional district $d$ in election year $e$:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:er}
    P^{ER}_{d,e} = s_{D,d,e} \cdot s_{R,d,e}
    \cdot \left(s_{D,d,e}^\alpha + s_{R,d,e}^\alpha\right), \quad \alpha = 1,
\end{equation}
which simplifies to $s_{D} \cdot s_{R}$ when $\alpha=1$, maximized at 0.25 when
the two parties split the vote equally. We aggregate to the state level weighting
by each district's share of total state two-party votes, forward-fill to annual
frequency, and standardize to mean zero and unit variance.

\subsection{Empirical Specification}

The unit of observation is an auditor change event. We estimate:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:main}
    Y_i = \alpha + \beta\,\textit{Pol}_{s(i),\,t(i)}
    + \gamma' X_{i,\,t(i)-1} + \tau_{t(i)} + \delta_{j(i)} + \varepsilon_i,
\end{equation}
where $Y_i \in \{|CAR_i|,\; AbVol_i\}$; $\textit{Pol}_{s(i),t(i)}$ is the
standardized ER polarization measure for firm $i$'s HQ state $s(i)$ in event
year $t(i)$; $\tau_{t(i)}$ are event-year fixed effects; $\delta_{j(i)}$ are
2-digit SIC industry fixed effects; and $X_{i,t(i)-1}$ includes size, leverage,
ROA, book-to-market, loss indicator, and sales growth, all measured in the fiscal
year prior to the event. Standard errors are clustered at the firm level.
The coefficient of interest is $\beta$.

\subsection{Identification}

Identification exploits two sources of variation in the polarization measure:
(i) cross-sectional variation across states in a given year, and (ii)
within-state time-series variation across election cycles. Year fixed effects
absorb aggregate shocks affecting all firms simultaneously. Industry fixed
effects control for systematic differences in auditor change patterns and market
reactions across sectors. The key identifying assumption is that, conditional on
controls and fixed effects, the polarization level of a firm's HQ state is
uncorrelated with unobserved event-specific factors that drive market reactions.
We provide supporting evidence via placebo tests in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

% ── 7. Conclusion ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Political polarization affects how investors interpret auditor signals, with
implications for market efficiency and audit regulation. Identical disclosures
generate more extreme market reactions --- larger absolute returns and higher
trading volume --- in more polarized political environments. These effects are
stronger for ambiguous signals, consistent with a model in which polarization
increases prior dispersion and amplifies the role of priors in belief updating.

The findings suggest that the effectiveness of disclosure-based regulation
depends on the political context in which disclosures are received. As
polarization has increased persistently over the past two decades, its effect
on audit signal interpretation may be growing. Future research should examine
whether these patterns extend to other audit-related disclosures, including
critical audit matters, going concern opinions, and internal control reports.
