% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{abstract}
We document that political polarization is associated with stronger
disagreement-sensitive market reactions to auditor-change disclosures.
Auditor-change filings (Form~8-K Item~4.01) are standardized mandatory
disclosures, yet they often contain qualitative causal explanations that
investors must interpret. In a sample of 678 auditor-change disclosures
for CRSP/Compustat-listed firms (2001--2023), firms headquartered in more
politically competitive states --- where the presidential electorate is
more evenly divided, our proxy for political polarization --- experience
significantly larger abnormal trading volume ($p = 0.010$) and absolute
abnormal returns ($p = 0.039$) around the filing. The effect is absent
for the same firms' earnings announcements ($N = 20{,}800$) and
officer-change disclosures ($N = 3{,}677$), and is concentrated in
filings that provide explicit causal explanations for the auditor change
--- consistent with disagreement arising when investors must interpret
substantive disclosure content rather than process numerically precise
signals. These findings suggest a limit to disclosure-based regulation:
standardized mandatory disclosure may not eliminate disagreement when the
filing is interpretation-intensive and investors process it through
different priors.
\end{abstract}

% ── 1. Introduction ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Auditor-change disclosures are among the most tightly regulated content-specific
disclosures in the Form~8-K system. Form~8-K Item~4.01 and the related
Regulation~S-K Item~304 require the registrant to disclose whether the
auditor resigned or was dismissed, any disagreements with the departing
auditor over accounting principles or auditing procedures, any reportable
events concerning the reliability of prior audits or internal controls, and
whether the audit committee approved the change. The former auditor must
furnish a concurrence letter to the SEC. This disclosure regime is
\emph{about} the audit function: it reveals information about the credibility
of the firm's financial reporting gatekeeper at a specific moment of
separation. Prior research establishes that these disclosures move prices:
abnormal returns around auditor changes reflect investor assessments of
reporting quality, audit risk, and the nature of the separation
\citep{johnson1990, dodd1984, defond1998, shu2000, landsman2009,
whisenant2003, hennes2014}. Yet the magnitude of market reactions varies
substantially across otherwise similar filings, and the sources of that
cross-sectional variation remain incompletely understood. This paper
documents a previously unexplored correlate: the political-information
environment in which investors interpret the disclosure. We find that
market reactions to Item~4.01 filings are larger where the firm's
headquarters state is more politically divided, consistent with investors
holding more heterogeneous priors about audit credibility and regulatory
oversight of the audit function.

Item~4.01 disclosures are distinctive among mandatory filings for three
reasons that make them a natural accounting research laboratory. First,
the content is \emph{regulated}: unlike press releases or management forecasts,
Reg~S-K Item~304 prescribes what must be disclosed, and the former auditor
must respond in writing. This removes the confound of selective disclosure
and standardizes the information set across firms. Second, the signal is
\emph{about audit credibility}: investors are asked to update their beliefs
about the reliability of the firm's reporting gatekeeper, not about a
typical earnings surprise. Assessing that update requires investors to
evaluate the credibility of the departing auditor, the incoming auditor,
and the regulatory infrastructure --- the PCAOB, audit standards, and the
broader institutional apparatus that underwrites audit quality. Third,
the signal is \emph{inherently ambiguous}: firms may change auditors for
reasons ranging from routine fee negotiation to concealed disagreements
over financial reporting, and the filing frequently provides incomplete
guidance about which explanation applies \citep{boone2001, beneish2005}.
Investors must form their own interpretation. Together, these features
make Item~4.01 a particularly clean setting for studying whether
politically polarized environments generate disagreement around
standardized but interpretation-intensive disclosures. The
audit-credibility dimension --- the fact that the signal requires
investors to assess the trustworthiness of auditors and the PCAOB as
institutions --- provides a theoretically motivated reason why this
setting may be especially likely to exhibit the pattern, though the
empirical design does not isolate this channel from other sources of
heterogeneous interpretation.

Our theoretical framework builds on \citet{kandel1995}: investors observe the
same public audit signal but may form different posteriors depending on their
priors about firm quality and the interpretation of qualitative disclosure
content. If political polarization amplifies this heterogeneity --- because
polarized investors may process the same substantive filing content through
different priors about what the auditor change portends --- then standard
belief-driven trading models \citep{harris1993} predict that greater
posterior dispersion translates into higher abnormal volume and larger
absolute price movements around the disclosure.

We examine this prediction using cross-state variation in partisan polarization,
measured as one minus the absolute two-party presidential vote margin at the
state level, constructed from MIT Election Lab county-level returns and
forward-filled to annual frequency.
We use presidential rather than congressional returns because presidential
elections are the most nationalized arena of U.S. electoral competition ---
outcomes are driven by broad national partisan forces rather than candidate-
or district-specific factors --- making them a cleaner proxy for the partisan
belief environment \citep{vertz1987, abramowitz2016, moskowitz2021}.
More polarized states --- where the presidential electorate is more evenly
divided between the parties --- capture political-information environments in
which investors may be more likely to hold divergent priors about institutional
credibility and the interpretation of regulatory signals. We acknowledge a key
limitation of this proxy: institutional investors are geographically dispersed
and need not share the political characteristics of the firm's headquarters
state. We treat headquarters-state partisan polarization as a noisy
reduced-form proxy for the political-information environment surrounding the
disclosure, not a precise measure of individual
investor beliefs; measurement error
of this kind attenuates estimated coefficients toward zero, so our findings are
likely conservative. Our sample consists of 678 Form~8-K Item~4.01 filings from
2001 to 2023, matched to CRSP daily return and volume data and Compustat firm
characteristics. Our main specification regresses abnormal
trading volume and $|CAR[-1,+1]|$ on standardized partisan polarization, controlling for firm size,
leverage, profitability, and book-to-market ratio, with year and industry
fixed effects throughout.

We find that firms headquartered in more politically competitive states experience
significantly larger abnormal trading volume ($p = 0.010$) and absolute abnormal
returns ($p = 0.039$) around auditor-change disclosures. Within-firm placebo
tests on the same firms' earnings announcements ($N = 20{,}800$) and
officer-change disclosures ($N = 3{,}677$) are inconsistent with a generic
salience explanation. The volume effect is concentrated in filings that provide
an explicit causal explanation for the auditor change ($p = 0.013$ for the
interaction), consistent with disagreement arising from the interpretation of
substantive disclosure content. The volume result is robust to three alternative
polarization proxies, alternative clustering structures, and a small-firm
subsample restriction; the absolute return result is more fragile across proxies,
consistent with volume being the sharper formal prediction.

Our contribution is to the auditor-change disclosure literature
specifically. A long literature establishes that auditor-change 8-Ks are
informative events whose content matters for market reactions
\citep{johnson1990, dodd1984, defond1998, shu2000, landsman2009,
whisenant2003, hennes2014}; that work asks whether the filing is
informative and which content drives the reaction. We add to this
literature by documenting that \emph{market reactions} to the same
Item~4.01 filing vary with the political environment surrounding the
disclosure --- state political competitiveness --- a cross-sectional
dimension not captured by existing firm-level or event-type moderators,
with the cleanest evidence in abnormal trading volume. Two within-firm
placebo tests indicate that this pattern is specific to auditor-change
disclosures: headquarters-state polarization has no detectable effect on
the same firms' earnings announcements ($N = 20{,}800$) or
officer-change (Item~5.02) disclosures ($N = 3{,}677$). Within the
auditor-change sample, the effect is concentrated in filings that
provide explicit causal explanations for the change, consistent with
disagreement arising from the interpretation of substantive disclosure
content rather than from a generic state-level characteristic. More
broadly, the findings suggest a modest limitation of disclosure-based
regulation: standardized mandatory disclosure may not eliminate
disagreement when the filing is interpretation-intensive and investors
process it through different priors.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{sec:lit} reviews
the related literature. Section~\ref{sec:theory} develops the theoretical
framework and states the hypothesis.
Section~\ref{sec:design} describes the empirical design. Section~\ref{sec:results}
presents the results. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

% ── 2. Related Literature ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Related Literature}
\label{sec:lit}

This paper sits at the intersection of three accounting and finance
conversations: how auditor-change disclosures inform capital markets,
how investor heterogeneity shapes disclosure interpretation, and how
political identity affects trust in audit institutions. We organize the
review around each conversation and locate our contribution within it.

\subsection{Auditor-Change Disclosures and Market Reactions}

A large accounting literature establishes that auditor-change disclosures
are informative events. The key findings, organized by what they establish
about the information content of these filings, are as follows.

\emph{Market reactions depend on the nature of the separation.}
\citet{dodd1984} document differential investor responses depending on
the disclosed circumstances. \citet{johnson1990} show that Big-8 departures
generate negative abnormal returns while Big-8 appointments are viewed
favorably. \citet{shu2000} identifies the particular informativeness of
\emph{resignations}: voluntarily departing auditors signal elevated client
risk that investors partially price at the filing date. \citet{hennes2014}
show that dismissals after accounting restatements signal different things
than resignations.

\emph{The content of the filing matters beyond the fact of the change.}
\citet{whisenant2003} examine Item~304 disclosures --- disagreements,
reportable events, and auditor concurrence letters --- and show that the
market responds to the disclosed content, not merely the announcement.
\citet{defond1998} find that discretionary accruals shift around auditor
changes in directions consistent with opinion shopping, suggesting that
investors use these filings to update about reporting quality.
\citet{landsman2009} show that the identities of the outgoing and incoming
auditors convey information about client risk realignment.

\emph{Filings often leave investors with substantial interpretive latitude.}
\citet{beneish2005} ask whether resignation filings reduce uncertainty
about reporting quality and conclude that they do not, because the
language of the filing often provides incomplete guidance about the
cause of the separation. \citet{boone2001} document that resignations
generate abnormal retail trading and reduced liquidity, suggesting that
unsophisticated investors face particular difficulty interpreting these
filings.

This literature answers two questions: \emph{are} auditor-change
disclosures informative? (yes) and \emph{which content} drives the
reaction? (disagreements, quality-tier transitions, stated reasons).
What it does not address is a third question: \emph{who interprets the
same filing differently, and why?} Our contribution is to show that
market reactions vary systematically with the political composition of
the local information environment --- a cross-sectional dimension that
is orthogonal to the filing-content moderators studied in the existing
literature.

\subsection{Investor Heterogeneity, Beliefs, and Disclosure Interpretation}

Models of heterogeneous beliefs provide the theoretical foundation for
our predictions. \citet{harris1993} show that differences of opinion
generate trade even without private information. \citet{kandel1995}
demonstrate that investors with heterogeneous priors interpret identical
public signals differently, generating trading volume and price effects
--- a result that maps directly to our setting, where the same Item~4.01
filing is received by all investors simultaneously. \citet{hong2007}
survey the broader disagreement-and-asset-prices literature.

A separate strand identifies political identity as a measurable source
of investor belief heterogeneity. \citet{kempf2024} show that partisan
identity systematically shapes financial attitudes and behavior.
\citet{gerberhuber2009} and \citet{coibion2020} document that political
beliefs affect economic expectations; \citet{barrioshochberg2021} show
similar effects in institutional investor behavior. Most closely related,
\citet{goldmanetal2024} find that politically slanted media coverage
generates disagreement and trading volume for politically salient firms.

We connect these two strands in a setting that differs from
\citet{goldmanetal2024} in two respects. First, our information set is a
standardized mandatory filing received identically by all investors, not
media-intermediated coverage whose framing varies across outlets. Second,
the object of investor belief is not a partisan policy outcome but the
credibility of a regulated gatekeeper relationship --- a dimension where
institutional trust, not policy preference, drives belief heterogeneity.
By linking the disagreement framework to a specific, measurable source of
prior heterogeneity in a clean disclosure setting, we provide a new
empirical context for understanding when and why the same public signal
generates divergent investor reactions.

\subsection{Political Economy and Credibility of Audit Institutions}
\label{sec:pol_markets}

A related literature examines how political factors influence the
\emph{supply} of audit quality. \citet{chan2006} document that political
connections shape auditor reporting behavior and switching in a cross-country
setting. \citet{lennox2010} show that Big~4 auditor oversight is associated
with lower fraud incidence, underscoring that the institutional
infrastructure surrounding auditing has measurable consequences for
financial reporting quality.

Our paper examines a different and largely unexplored dimension: the
\emph{demand side} --- how investors with different levels of institutional
trust process audit signals. This dimension is particularly salient for
Item~4.01 disclosures because of two institutional features unique to this
setting. First, the former auditor must furnish a concurrence letter to the
SEC, a regulatory mechanism designed to impose credibility on the
disclosure; investors who trust the PCAOB's enforcement apparatus may view
this letter as a meaningful check, while investors skeptical of regulatory
institutions may discount it. Second, the PCAOB's authority and credibility
as a regulatory body may not be assessed uniformly across investor groups:
the affective-polarization literature documents that partisan identity
systematically shapes trust in governmental and regulatory institutions
\citep{iyengar2012, iyengar2019}, and \citet{kempf2024} show that this
extends to financial attitudes and behavior.

This investor-side mechanism is orthogonal to the auditor-side political
economy literature and complements it: even if political pressures leave
auditor behavior unchanged, polarization in the investor population can
amplify the capital-market consequences of the same disclosure. Our
contribution to this conversation is to show that the effectiveness of
audit-related mandatory disclosure varies with the political-information
environment in which investors interpret it.

% ── 3. Theory ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Theory}
\label{sec:theory}

This section presents a stylized model that motivates the reduced-form
predictions tested below. The model is not structurally estimated; its
purpose is to formalize the intuition that polarization increases
disagreement around credibility-sensitive disclosures and to discipline
the choice of empirical outcomes. The Bayesian framework operationalizes
belief heterogeneity through prior beliefs, which is the most tractable
formal channel. Institutional trust and perceived signal precision
generate the same comparative statics and should be understood as
alternative sources of prior-like heterogeneity (see
Appendix~\ref{app:proofs} for formal statements). The model thus
provides a stylized microfoundation, not a claim that priors are the
only operative channel.

In the affective-polarization literature \citep{iyengar2012,
iyengar2019}, partisan identity and out-party distrust shape how individuals
evaluate the credibility of information and the institutions that produce it.
In the present setting, these forces can be interpreted as widening the
cross-group gap in trust in the disclosure ($\tau_g$) or in perceived
signal precision ($\kappa_g$). As shown in Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}, all
three channels generate the same posterior-dispersion comparative static:
whenever polarization widens the cross-group posterior log-odds index, belief
dispersion and disagreement-sensitive market outcomes strictly increase. The
heterogeneous-priors model below should therefore be read as a tractable
benchmark case of this broader class of polarization mechanisms.

\subsection{Setting}

The model draws on the heterogeneous-priors framework of \citet{kandel1995}.
Consider a firm whose true quality $\theta \in \{0, 1\}$ is unknown to
investors. The firm discloses an auditor-related signal $s \in \{0, 1\}$ (e.g.,
an auditor change announcement). Signals are informative: $P(s=1 \mid
\theta=1) = p_H > P(s=1 \mid \theta=0) = p_L$, with $p_H > p_L$.

\textbf{Mapping to Item~4.01.} In our setting, the signal $s$ corresponds
to the content of the auditor-change filing: whether the auditor resigned
or was dismissed, whether disagreements or reportable events are disclosed,
and the quality-tier direction of the transition. The signal precision
parameters $\{p_H, p_L\}$ reflect how sharply the filing's content
discriminates between high and low firm quality --- a function of the
specific disclosure requirements of Reg~S-K Item~304. The former auditor's
concurrence letter, which must be furnished to the SEC, is a regulatory
mechanism designed to constrain the informativeness of the signal by
requiring the departing auditor to confirm or dispute the registrant's
characterization. In the model, this institutional apparatus enters
through the likelihood parameters: a fully credible concurrence letter
narrows $\{p_H, p_L\}$, while investors who discount its reliability
effectively face a noisier signal.

Investors observe $s$ and form posterior beliefs. All investors share the
same likelihood function $\{p_H, p_L\}$ but differ in their prior beliefs
about the firm's quality.

\textbf{Two-group structure.} We model investors as belonging to two
ideological groups indexed by political affiliation $g \in \{D, R\}$, with
population shares $\omega_D$ and $\omega_R = 1 - \omega_D$. Group $g$ holds
prior $\pi_g \equiv P_g(\theta = 1) \in (0,1)$, with $\pi_D \neq \pi_R$.
Group-level priors reflect differential trust in auditors and regulatory
institutions. In the model, all heterogeneity is captured by differences
in $\pi_g$; in practice, polarization may also affect how investors perceive
signal precision ($p_H, p_L$) or how much weight they place on the disclosure
relative to their priors. The reduced-form predictions are the same: greater
political distance generates greater dispersion in posterior beliefs.
Polarization increases the \emph{dispersion} of priors across groups without
changing the \emph{average} prior. In the Item~4.01 context, this divergence
has a specific institutional source: the PCAOB's credibility as the oversight
body that underwrites the audit function. Investors who trust the PCAOB's
enforcement apparatus treat auditor-change disclosures as credible signals
about reporting quality; investors skeptical of regulatory institutions
discount the same filings, forming priors that are farther from the
signal's informational content.

\subsection{Belief Updating}

Group $g$'s posterior belief after observing $s=1$ is:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:bayes}
    P_g(\theta = 1 \mid s = 1)
    = \frac{p_H \, \pi_g}{p_H \, \pi_g + p_L (1 - \pi_g)}
    \equiv \phi(\pi_g).
\end{equation}
The function $\phi(\cdot)$ is strictly increasing in $\pi_g$. It is also concave
(since $\phi''(\pi) < 0$ for all $\pi \in (0,1)$ when $p_H > p_L$); concavity
implies that a mean-preserving spread of priors shifts the average posterior
downward, which has implications for equilibrium prices, but it is not required
for the variance result below, which depends only on strict monotonicity.
In the Item~4.01 setting, the monotonicity of $\phi$ has a concrete
interpretation: an investor who enters the disclosure with a higher prior
about the firm's reporting quality (e.g., because she trusts the PCAOB's
oversight of the departing auditor) leaves the disclosure with a higher
posterior. The concurrence letter and the regulated structure of the filing
do not eliminate this gap --- they merely standardize the signal that
both groups observe. The gap in posteriors therefore persists as long
as the gap in priors does.

We now establish the central comparative static of the model: that any
widening of the gap between group priors --- holding the average prior
fixed --- increases the dispersion of posterior beliefs. This is the
formal sense in which polarization amplifies disagreement.

\begin{proposition}[Polarization and belief dispersion]
    \label{prop:dispersion}
    Fix population shares $\omega_D, \omega_R > 0$ with $\omega_D + \omega_R = 1$ and mean prior
    $\bar{\pi} = \omega_D \pi_D + \omega_R \pi_R$. The variance of posterior beliefs
    across groups is
    \[
        \mathrm{Var}[\phi(\pi_g)]
        = \omega_D \omega_R \bigl(\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R)\bigr)^2.
    \]
    For any increase in $|\pi_D - \pi_R|$ that holds $\bar{\pi}$ fixed,
    $\mathrm{Var}[\phi(\pi_g)]$ strictly increases.
\end{proposition}

\noindent\textit{Proof.} See Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}. $\square$

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Remark.} Proposition~\ref{prop:dispersion} isolates the
mechanism through which polarization enters the model. The key condition is
a \emph{mean-preserving spread} of priors: polarization shifts the two groups
farther apart while leaving the population-average prior $\bar{\pi}$ unchanged.
This rules out the alternative that polarization merely correlates with
aggregate pessimism or optimism about firm quality. Under this condition, the
posterior variance increases strictly --- more polarized priors generate more
dispersed beliefs after observing the same signal. The proposition thereby provides the formal microfoundation for the primary
empirical prediction: more dispersed posteriors produce higher
disagreement-driven trading volume (Prediction~1). Larger absolute price
movements (Prediction~2) follow from the same dispersion mechanism under
additional market-friction assumptions but are not formally derived here.
The result is stated for a tractable two-group setting; extension to
richer prior distributions would require additional assumptions on the
shape of the distribution and is left for future work.

\subsection{Equilibrium Prices and Trading}

Trading volume is increasing in posterior belief dispersion
\citep{harris1993, kandel1995}:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:volume}
    \frac{\partial\, V}{\partial\, \mathrm{Var}[\phi(\pi_g)]} > 0.
\end{equation}

By Proposition~\ref{prop:dispersion}, polarization strictly increases
posterior belief dispersion. Trading volume is the outcome most directly
linked to this result: more dispersed posteriors generate more investor
pairs with divergent beliefs and hence mutually beneficial trading
opportunities, raising aggregate volume even in the absence of private
information.

\paragraph{From posterior dispersion to absolute returns.}
The mapping from posterior-belief dispersion to trading volume is direct:
when investors form more dispersed posteriors after the same public signal,
more pairs of investors have opposing desired trades, increasing turnover.
The link to absolute abnormal returns requires one additional
market-clearing ingredient. Suppose investor $i$ demands
\[
    q_i(P) = \alpha_i\bigl(\mu_i - P\bigr),
\]
where $\mu_i$ is investor $i$'s posterior valuation and $\alpha_i > 0$
captures risk-bearing capacity. If pessimistic investors can short without
friction and all trades clear symmetrically, disagreement need not generate
large price movements even though it increases volume. By contrast, under
standard limits to arbitrage --- such as short-sale constraints,
heterogeneous risk-bearing capacity, or segmented participation
\citep{miller1977, hong2003, gromb2010} --- optimistic and pessimistic
demands do not offset one-for-one. In that case, greater posterior
dispersion generates larger net order imbalances and therefore larger
equilibrium price adjustments in absolute value. We therefore view $AbVol$
as the sharpest implication of the model, and $|CAR|$ as a secondary
outcome that follows under empirically plausible market-friction
assumptions.

\subsection{Role of Signal Ambiguity}
\label{sec:ambiguity}

The model suggests that polarization effects may be larger when audit
signals leave greater interpretive latitude. The intuition is
straightforward: when a filing provides clear guidance about the nature
of the auditor change (e.g., a disclosed disagreement or a transparent
quality-tier transition), investors' assessments of signal
informativeness converge regardless of political identity. When the
filing is ambiguous --- a lateral move with a generic or absent stated
reason --- investors must form their own assessments, and these may
diverge along politically shaped lines. The formal apparatus is in
Appendix~\ref{app:proofs} (Proposition~\ref{prop:precision}).

Empirically, ambiguity and economic materiality are difficult to
separate in this setting: events we classify as high-ambiguity tend
also to be less economically consequential. We construct a
\texttt{high\_ambiguity} indicator from observable filing
characteristics (defined in Section~\ref{sec:design}) and explore its
interaction with polarization, but the resulting tests are
statistically insignificant and we do not treat them as evidence on
mechanism.

\subsection{Testable Predictions}

\begin{itemize}
    \item[\textbf{P1.}] \textit{Polarization and trading volume (primary formal
    prediction).} By Proposition~\ref{prop:dispersion} and
    Equation~\eqref{eq:volume}, polarization increases posterior-belief
    variance, which increases disagreement-driven trading volume. This is
    the primary formal implication of the model. In the Item~4.01 setting,
    this prediction arises because politically heterogeneous investors
    may interpret the same qualitative filing content differently, forming
    divergent posteriors and creating mutually beneficial trading
    opportunities. We test P1 using abnormal
    trading volume ($AbVol$) as the outcome (defined in
    Section~\ref{sec:design}).

    \item[\textbf{P2.}] \textit{Polarization and absolute returns (secondary
    empirical prediction).} When investors on opposing sides of the prior
    distribution revise beliefs in opposite directions, absolute price
    movements are amplified even when the average signed reaction is close to
    zero. This prediction is consistent with the model's dispersion mechanism,
    but the link from posterior variance to $|CAR|$ depends additionally on
    demand asymmetry, short-sale constraints, or limits to arbitrage
    \citep{miller1977, hong2003, gromb2010}. We therefore treat $|CAR|$
    as a complementary empirical outcome: evidence that polarization increases
    both $AbVol$ and $|CAR|$ is more informative than either alone, but $AbVol$
    is the cleaner formal prediction. In this setting, the price effect
    captures the net impact on valuation when investors with divergent
    interpretations of the filing content trade against one another.
    We test P2 by regressing $|CAR[-1,+1]|$
    on the state-year polarization measure, with the same controls and fixed
    effects as P1.
\end{itemize}

The model also suggests that polarization effects may be stronger when
filings leave greater interpretive latitude (Section~\ref{sec:ambiguity}).
We explore this using filing-level informativeness measures, but the
interaction tests are statistically insignificant and we report them as
exploratory analyses in Appendix~\ref{app:tables}.

\textbf{Discussion.} The model formalizes heterogeneous priors, but the
same comparative statics arise under heterogeneous institutional trust
or perceived signal precision (Appendix~\ref{app:proofs},
Proposition~\ref{prop:unified}). The core prediction is therefore
channel-agnostic: polarization amplifies belief-dispersion-driven
outcomes regardless of which specific source of heterogeneity
predominates. The empirical design identifies disagreement around
credibility-sensitive disclosure but cannot distinguish among these
channels.

\subsection{Hypothesis}
\label{sec:hypotheses}

The theoretical framework yields one testable hypothesis:

\begin{hyp}
    \label{h1}
    Investor reactions to auditor-change disclosures are larger in magnitude
    in more politically polarized environments. Specifically, abnormal
    trading volume ($AbVol$) around auditor-change events is increasing in
    political polarization (primary formal prediction, per
    Proposition~\ref{prop:dispersion}), and absolute cumulative abnormal
    returns ($|CAR[-1,+1]|$) are increasing in polarization as a secondary
    empirical prediction.
\end{hyp}

We also examine, on an exploratory basis, whether the polarization
effect differs between auditor resignations and client-initiated
dismissals. Although resignations are worse-news signals on average
\citep{ghosh2015, beneish2005}, most resignation 8-Ks carry no
attribution to any accounting disagreement \citep{beneish2005} and do
not reduce information asymmetry \citep{boone2001}, leaving investors to
rely on their own priors. We treat event-type splits as descriptive
rather than as a core hypothesis.

% ── 4. Empirical Design ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Empirical Design}
\label{sec:design}

\subsection{Sample Construction}

\textbf{Auditor change events.} We identify auditor changes using Form~8-K
filings disclosing a change in the registrant's certifying accountant (Item
4.01), obtained from SEC EDGAR for 2001--2023.\footnote{We end the sample in
2023, the last full year for which DW-NOMINATE ideological scores are
available in final form from Voteview. Scores for the 119th Congress (which
began January 2025) have not yet been finalized at the time of writing. The
Esteban-Ray electoral measure covers the same period, as MIT Election Lab
House returns are available through the 2022 midterm election, which is
forward-filled to cover calendar years through 2023.} We retain only
original 8-K filings (excluding 1,720 amendments) matched to a CRSP PERMNO
and require at least 100 non-missing trading days in the estimation window.
Table~\ref{tab:attrition} reports the full attrition. Of 6,901 original
filings, 5,628 cannot be linked to a CRSP/Compustat security --- primarily
because the filers are micro-cap or OTC registrants not covered by
CRSP. Panel B of Table~\ref{tab:attrition} compares observable
characteristics of matched and unmatched filings. Disagreement rates are
broadly similar (4.6\% vs.\ 4.6\%); the primary difference is firm size,
proxied by Big~4 auditor involvement (43\% vs.\ 14\%). The matched sample
is therefore tilted toward larger, exchange-listed firms --- a standard
feature of CRSP/Compustat-based research designs in the auditing literature
\citep{defond2014}. Because our theory operates through investor belief
heterogeneity rather than firm size per se, this selection is conservative:
to the extent that larger firms have more dispersed and sophisticated
investor bases, the local-investor channel should be \emph{weaker}, not
stronger, in the matched sample. The final sample consists
of 678 auditor-change events for 456 unique firms after excluding financial
firms (SIC 6000--6999), utilities (SIC 4900--4999), and events with missing
Compustat controls or headquarters-state
information.\footnote{Of the 1,220 CRSP-matched events, 536 are dropped
for missing Compustat controls or headquarters-state information
(primarily firms with no Compustat financial records despite possessing a
CRSP--Compustat link key), and 6 are dropped by the financial and utility
industry screens (SIC 4900--4999, 6000--6999). The attrition is therefore
driven almost entirely by data availability, not researcher discretion.}
Because CRSP/Compustat coverage is concentrated among exchange-listed
firms, our results should be interpreted as applying to the listed-firm
segment of auditor-change events rather than to the full population of
Item~4.01 filings.

\subsection{Dependent Variables}

\textbf{Abnormal trading volume ($AbVol$).} We measure trading-volume
reactions over the $[-1, +1]$ event window, consistent with the short-run
agreement-to-disagree framework of \citet{kandel1995} in which volume spikes
at the moment beliefs diverge. Define $LV_{e,t} \equiv \log(\mathrm{Vol}_{e,t}+1)$
and let $\overline{LV}_e$ denote its mean over the estimation window
$[-252, -46]$. Then:
\[
    AbVol_e = \frac{1}{3}\sum_{t=-1}^{+1}\bigl(LV_{e,t} - \overline{LV}_e\bigr).
\]

\textbf{Absolute cumulative abnormal return ($|CAR|$).} Following standard
event-study methodology \citep{mackinlay1997}, we estimate a market model
over the estimation window $[-252, -46]$ trading days relative to the event
date, using at least 100 non-missing trading days to estimate parameters:
\begin{equation}
    AR_{e,t} = R_{e,t} - (\hat{\alpha}_e + \hat{\beta}_e \cdot R_{m,t}),
    \quad t \in \{-1, 0, +1\}.
\end{equation}
The window begins 252 days before the event to capture a full trading year
of pre-event returns and ends 46 days before to avoid contamination from
information leakage in the run-up period. $CAR_e[-1,+1] = \sum_{t=-1}^{+1} AR_{e,t}$
and $|CAR_e[-1,+1]|$ is a secondary dependent variable. We use the CRSP
value-weighted return as the market return and include delisting returns
following \citet{beaver2007}.

\subsection{Political Polarization Measure}

A central empirical challenge is that the theory's polarization object ---
the latent force that increases divergence in interpretation through identity,
alienation, and perceived institutional credibility --- is not directly
observable at the investor level \citep{duclos2004}. The available data
provide only grouped or indirect proxies, each of which captures a different
margin of the latent concept \citep{esteban1994, esteban2007}. We therefore examine three
complementary proxies rather than treating any single measure as definitive.
The state-level presidential polarization measure below captures
\emph{cross-sectional partisan division}; DW-NOMINATE captures
\emph{elite ideological distance}; and the within-state county vote-share
standard deviation captures \emph{geographic partisan sorting}. These measure different aspects of the
political landscape \citep{wang2000}.\footnote{%
The continuous-density estimators developed by \citet{duclos2004} provide
the most rigorous formalization of the identity--alienation concept, but
require a directly observed distribution of the underlying investor-level
trait. Because our setting does not yield a firm-year density of investor
political identity, we use reduced-form political-environment proxies instead.}

We measure state-level partisan polarization from county-level presidential
election returns (MIT Election Lab, 2000--2020). For state $s$ and presidential
election year $t^{*}$, let $D_{s,t^{*}}$ and $R_{s,t^{*}} = 1 - D_{s,t^{*}}$ denote the
Democratic and Republican two-party vote shares aggregated across counties.
The partisan margin is $M_{s,t^{*}} = |D_{s,t^{*}} - R_{s,t^{*}}|$, ranging from zero in
perfectly contested states to one in completely one-sided states. We define
\emph{partisan polarization} as:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:comp}
    \text{Polarization}_{s,t^{*}} = 1 - M_{s,t^{*}} = 1 - |D_{s,t^{*}} - R_{s,t^{*}}|,
\end{equation}
which is maximized at 1.0 when both parties split the vote equally and
minimized at 0 in one-party states. Higher values indicate a more evenly
divided electorate, which we interpret as reflecting greater partisan
polarization in the investor base. More polarized
states are politically divided environments where investors are more
likely to hold heterogeneous priors
about institutional trustworthiness and the interpretation of regulatory
signals.\footnote{We also test the Esteban-Ray polarization index
$P^{ER}_{s,t^{*}} = D_{s,t^{*}} \cdot R_{s,t^{*}}$ and a congressional-district
ER index as robustness checks (Table~\ref{tab:robustness}, columns~2--3).
The ER index has lower discriminating power away from the 50-50 split
(SD 0.027 vs.\ 0.101 for the margin); the congressional-district variant
is further compressed by gerrymandering (SD 0.011). We prefer presidential
returns because they are the most nationalized arena of U.S. electoral
competition \citep{vertz1987, abramowitz2016}, providing a cleaner proxy
for the broad partisan cleavage that we hypothesize shapes investor
belief heterogeneity.}

We forward-fill each presidential election's measure over the four subsequent
calendar years --- assigning the 2000 vote to 2001--2004, the 2004 vote to
2005--2008, and so on through the 2020 election, which covers 2021--2023
within our sample period. This assignment assumes that the state political
environment evolves at the electoral-cycle frequency rather than annually. We standardize to mean zero and unit variance
within the analysis sample. Because different polarization indices emphasize
different aspects of between-group separation and within-group cohesion, the
polarization measure cannot be treated as exhaustive \citep{wang2000}.
A complementary measure based on elite roll-call behavior is therefore needed.

As a second proxy for polarization, we construct a measure of elite ideological
distance from DW-NOMINATE first-dimension scores \citep{poole1997,poole2007}.
DW-NOMINATE scores capture legislators' revealed ideological positions from
roll-call voting records, rather than electoral outcomes, and thus measure
\emph{ideological} polarization directly. For each state $s$ and Congress $c$,
we compute:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:dw}
    P^{DW}_{s,c} = \overline{\text{dim1}}_{R,s,c}
    - \overline{\text{dim1}}_{D,s,c},
\end{equation}
where $\overline{\text{dim1}}_{R,s,c}$ and $\overline{\text{dim1}}_{D,s,c}$
are the mean first-dimension DW-NOMINATE scores among House Republicans and
Democrats from state $s$ in Congress $c$, respectively. Because the first
dimension captures the liberal--conservative continuum, $P^{DW}_{s,c}$ is
always positive and increasing in ideological distance between the parties.
We expand each Congress's scores to the two calendar years it covers and
standardize to mean zero and unit variance. The national-level counterpart,
$P^{DW\text{-nat}}_{c}$, computes the same gap across all House members
nationally and varies only over time, providing a pure time-series check.
The electoral polarization and DW-NOMINATE measures are complementary proxies:
electoral polarization captures division in the mass electorate, while
DW-NOMINATE captures ideological distance among elected representatives.
If both predict the same investor response, the evidence for a polarization
mechanism is substantially stronger.

As a third proxy, we construct the population-weighted within-state standard
deviation of county-level Democratic vote shares. This \emph{county vote SD}
measure captures geographic partisan sorting: a high value indicates that a
state contains both deep-red and deep-blue counties, consistent with genuine
local polarization, while a low value indicates counties that are uniformly
similar. Crucially, this measure addresses the concern, raised by
\citet{fiorina2005}, that electoral competitiveness may reflect moderate
swing voters rather than polarized blocs: a state can be 50-50 and yet
have low county-level variance if all its counties vote near 50-50.

\smallskip
\noindent\textit{Limitations of measurement.} Each proxy is subject to
known limitations. The presidential polarization measure assigns equal
weight to all counties within a state regardless of firm location, and
forward-filling assumes no within-cycle variation. The state-level DW-NOMINATE
gap may reflect party sorting rather than genuine ideological movement and
is available only at biennial frequency.

\subsection{Cross-Sectional Variables}

\textbf{Signal ambiguity.} We construct a binary \texttt{high\_ambiguity}
indicator for exploratory cross-sectional tests. An event is classified as high-ambiguity if the
filing discloses no formal disagreement between the registrant and the
departing auditor \emph{and} the auditor change does not involve a clear
quality-tier transition (i.e., the change is lateral --- Big-4-to-Big-4 or
non-Big-4-to-non-Big-4 --- or the quality direction is unclassified).
High-ambiguity events comprise 395 of the 678 sample observations (58.3\%).

\textbf{Event type.} We classify each event as a \emph{dismissal} (the client
terminated the auditor) or \emph{non-dismissal} (the auditor resigned, declined
to stand for re-election, or the separation is otherwise not client-initiated),
based on the language of the 8-K filing.

\subsection{Empirical Specification}

The unit of observation is an auditor-change event $e$. We estimate:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:main}
    Y_e = \alpha + \beta\,\textit{Pol}_{s(e),\,t(e)}
    + \gamma' Z_{e,\,t(e)-1}
    + \lambda_{t(e)} + \delta_{j(e)} + \varepsilon_e,
\end{equation}
where $Y_e$ is $AbVol_e$ or $|CAR_e[-1,+1]|$; $\textit{Pol}_{s(e),t(e)}$ is the
standardized state political polarization measure for the headquarters state
$s(e)$ of the event's firm in event year $t(e)$;
$Z_{e,t(e)-1}$ is a vector of firm controls measured in the fiscal year prior
to the event (size, leverage, ROA, book-to-market, loss indicator, and sales
growth); $\lambda_{t(e)}$ are event-year fixed effects; and $\delta_{j(e)}$ are
two-digit SIC industry fixed effects. Standard errors are clustered at the firm
level. The coefficient of interest is $\beta$.

\subsection{Identification}

Headquarters-state polarization is a reduced-form proxy, not a direct
measure of investor beliefs. The inferential chain from theory to
empirics requires several links: state political competitiveness shapes
the local political-information environment; the local environment
shapes investor priors about firm quality and the interpretation of
qualitative disclosure content; heterogeneous priors generate
heterogeneous interpretations of Item~4.01 filings; and heterogeneous
interpretations produce disagreement-sensitive market outcomes. What is
directly observed is only the first and last elements of this chain:
headquarters-state political competitiveness and market reactions. The
intermediate links --- that specific investors hold politically shaped
priors, and that these priors drive trading --- are consistent with the
theory and the placebo evidence but are not individually identified.
The paper therefore documents an association between the political
environment surrounding the disclosure and disagreement-sensitive
market outcomes, shows that this association is specific to
auditor-change disclosures, and provides within-filing evidence that
the effect is concentrated in filings with substantive causal content.
It does not identify which investors trade or establish which precise
psychological channel operates.

The intended channel runs through the local information environment
surrounding the firm, particularly retail investors, who are more
geographically concentrated than institutional investors and more likely
to anchor their beliefs to local political norms \citep{coval1999}.
Consistent with this channel, \citet{boone2001} find that auditor
resignation announcements generate significant abnormal trading activity
among individual investors but not institutional investors. The
small-firm subsample test (Table~\ref{tab:robustness}, column~8)
provides a directional validation: the AbVol coefficient strengthens
($\beta = 0.137$, $p < 0.05$) for below-median-size firms, where the
headquarters-state proxy is better matched to the marginal investor
base. We also interact polarization with an above-median retail
ownership indicator (one minus 13F institutional ownership). The
interaction is directionally positive for both outcomes but
statistically insignificant ($p = 0.201$ for $|CAR|$; $p = 0.552$ for
AbVol). Combined with the small-firm result, these tests are suggestive
but not conclusive.

The main limitation is that equity investors are not confined to the
headquarters state. Institutional ownership is national, and
headquarters-state polarization captures the intended channel with error,
which attenuates estimated coefficients. Several robustness tests address
specific concerns.
State fixed effects (Table~\ref{tab:robustness}, column~4) absorb all
time-invariant cross-state differences in investor composition, media
environments, and regulatory climate. The DW-NOMINATE specification
(column~3) uses congressional roll-call votes rather than electoral
returns and still significantly predicts abnormal volume ($p < 0.10$).
A county-level polarization measure (column~7) and the within-state
standard deviation of county-level vote shares (column~9, $p = 0.056$)
are consistent with the result not being an artifact of geographic
aggregation or electoral closeness \citep{fiorina2005}. The within-firm
placebo tests
(Sections~\ref{sec:placebo} and~\ref{sec:placebo_502}) address the
concern that any state-level correlate --- including local media or
regulatory environment --- could drive the result: the effect is absent
for earnings announcements and officer-change disclosures from the same
firms. Because the polarization regressor varies at the
state$\times$election-cycle level, columns~(5)--(6) of
Table~\ref{tab:robustness} cluster at the state level and implement
two-way clustering by firm and state \citep{cameron2011}; results are
unchanged.

Finally, the theoretical framework is deliberately channel-agnostic:
heterogeneous priors, heterogeneous institutional trust, and heterogeneous
perceived signal precision all generate the same comparative static
(Proposition~\ref{prop:unified}). The empirical design therefore identifies
disagreement around credibility-sensitive disclosure in politically
competitive environments, but it cannot distinguish the specific
psychological channel through which polarization enters investor beliefs.

\subsection{Main Results}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports the baseline results. Columns (1)--(2) use
abnormal volume as the dependent variable (the primary formal prediction);
columns (3)--(4) use $|CAR[-1,+1]|$. Odd columns include year fixed effects
only; even columns add industry fixed effects and the full set of firm
controls. Across all four specifications, the coefficient on
\textit{Polarization} is positive and statistically significant, ranging
from $\beta = 0.119$ ($p = 0.006$) to $\beta = 0.111$ ($p = 0.010$) for
abnormal volume, and from $\beta = 0.006$ ($p = 0.015$) to
$\beta = 0.005$ ($p = 0.039$) for $|CAR|$.
The economic magnitude is meaningful: a one-standard-deviation increase in partisan
polarization is associated with a 0.11-unit increase in abnormal volume
and a 0.5 percentage point increase in $|CAR|$ relative to a sample mean of 5.0\%.
Notably, the volume result is stronger and more precisely estimated ($p = 0.010$)
than the absolute return result ($p = 0.039$). This ordering is predicted by
disagreement models \citep{kandel1995}: when investors receive the same signal
but form heterogeneous posteriors, the most direct consequence is increased
trading as optimists buy from pessimists; the effect on unsigned returns is a
secondary implication that depends on the relative wealth and aggressiveness of
disagreeing investor groups. The fact that the volume channel is more robust
than the price channel --- a pattern that extends to alternative
polarization proxies in Table~\ref{tab:robustness} --- is consistent with
the disagreement mechanism rather than generic salience, which would
predict at least as strong a price effect as a volume effect.

\subsection{Placebo Test: Earnings Announcements}
\label{sec:placebo}

A natural concern is that headquarters-state polarization simply amplifies
market reactions to any corporate news event, not specifically to disclosures
that require interpretation. We address this with a within-firm placebo test.
For the same 456 firms in our analysis sample, we identify all quarterly
earnings announcement dates (Compustat \texttt{rdq}) over the same 2001--2023
period, excluding any earnings announcement within 30 calendar days of an
auditor-change event to avoid contamination. This yields 20,800 placebo events.
We estimate a market model for each earnings event using the same methodology
as the main analysis (estimation window $[-252, -46]$ trading days, CRSP
value-weighted market return) and compute both $AbVol[-1,+1]$ and
$|CAR[-1,+1]|$.

Table~\ref{tab:placebo_8k} reports the results. Columns~(1)--(3) use
abnormal volume; columns~(4)--(6) use $|CAR|$. For AbVol, the
auditor-change baseline is $\beta = 0.111$ ($p < 0.01$, column~1), while
the earnings-announcement coefficient is $\beta = -0.010$ ($p > 0.10$,
column~2) --- a clean null with over 20,000 observations. The pooled
interaction (column~3) is $\beta = 0.141$ ($p < 0.01$), indicating that
the polarization effect on trading volume is significantly and
substantially larger for auditor-change disclosures than for earnings
announcements. The pattern is identical for $|CAR|$: a null placebo
coefficient ($\beta = -0.000$, $p > 0.10$, column~5) and a significant
pooled interaction ($\beta = 0.008$, $p < 0.01$, column~6). In both
cases, the base polarization coefficient in the pooled specification is
indistinguishable from zero, indicating that the entire effect is
concentrated in auditor changes.\footnote{The negative \textit{Auditor
Change} main effects ($-0.384$ for AbVol, $-0.024$ for $|CAR|$, both
$p < 0.01$) indicate that auditor-change events generate smaller average
market reactions than earnings announcements, consistent with the former
being less news-intensive than quarterly results.}

Earnings announcements carry unambiguous quantitative content --- the reported
EPS figure --- leaving far less room for politically shaped interpretation than
an auditor-change filing, which requires investors to evaluate auditor
credibility and infer the nature of the separation from limited disclosure. The
placebo null for both outcomes is consistent with the interpretation
channel but does not uniquely identify it: the null rules out generic
news amplification but is also consistent with polarization amplifying
reactions to any qualitative or governance-related disclosure, not
exclusively to audit-credibility-sensitive signals.\footnote{%
We also tested whether the polarization effect is amplified among
firms where audit credibility is plausibly more salient --- financially
distressed firms (below-median Altman Z-score), firms with high discretionary
accruals (above-median Modified Jones model $|DA|$), and firms whose prior-year
audit opinion included a going-concern modification. None of these firm-level
interactions is statistically significant ($p > 0.35$ in all cases;
see Table~\ref{tab:audit_credibility} in Appendix~\ref{app:tables}),
consistent with the effect being driven by the state-level
political-information environment rather than by firm-specific accounting
quality concerns.}

\subsection{Placebo Test: Officer-Change Disclosures}
\label{sec:placebo_502}

The earnings-announcement placebo is inconsistent with a generic salience explanation,
but it leaves open a tighter alternative: that polarization amplifies
reactions to \emph{any} mandatory 8-K filing requiring interpretation, not
specifically to audit-related disclosures. Form~8-K Item~5.02 officer-change
filings --- departures, elections, and appointments of directors and
certain officers --- provide the cleanest test of this alternative. Like
auditor-change filings, they are mandatory, standardized, and often require
investors to infer the nature of the change from limited disclosure
\citep{ghosh2015}. Unlike auditor-change filings, they do not involve the interpretation
of audit-specific qualitative content. If the polarization effect is
specific to audit disclosures rather than generic to any mandatory 8-K
filing requiring interpretation, it should be larger for auditor changes
than for officer changes from the same firms.

We identify Item~5.02 filings by parsing the EDGAR full-text search index
for the Item~5.02 title phrase and extracting the relevant 8-K sections
with \texttt{edgarParser}. Restricting to the 456 firms in our analysis
sample and excluding events within 30 calendar days of the same firm's
auditor-change event yields 3,677 placebo events. We estimate a market
model for each event using the same methodology as the main analysis and
compute $AbVol[-1,+1]$ and $|CAR[-1,+1]|$.

Table~\ref{tab:placebo_502} reports the results in the same format as the
earnings-announcement placebo (Table~\ref{tab:placebo_8k}). The pattern
is consistent across both outcomes. For AbVol, the auditor-change baseline
is $\beta = 0.111$ ($p < 0.01$, column~1), while the officer-change
coefficient is $\beta = 0.016$ ($p > 0.10$, column~2). The pooled
interaction (column~3) is $\beta = 0.110$ ($p = 0.011$). For $|CAR|$, the
placebo coefficient is null ($\beta = -0.000$, $p > 0.10$, column~5) and
the pooled interaction is $\beta = 0.006$ ($p = 0.006$, column~6). Both
pooled interactions indicate that the polarization effect is significantly
and substantially larger for auditor-change disclosures than for
officer-change disclosures from the same firms --- a tighter specificity
test than the earnings placebo, because both event types are mandatory
8-K filings requiring interpretation from limited disclosure. The
pattern is consistent with the effect being specific to auditor-change
disclosures rather than generic to any ambiguous mandatory filing. We
note, however, that the officer-change placebo does not fully isolate
why auditor-change filings exhibit this pattern: it remains possible
that polarization amplifies reactions to any qualitative governance
disclosure, not specifically to audit-related content. The within-filing
evidence on explicit causal content (Section~\ref{sec:exploratory})
provides additional specificity, but the precise channel through which
auditor-change filings generate politically mediated disagreement is not
identified.

\subsection{Robustness and Alternative Proxies}

Table~\ref{tab:robustness} addresses conventional robustness concerns and
examines whether the baseline result extends to alternative polarization
proxies. Panel~A reports results for abnormal volume (the primary formal
prediction); Panel~B reports $|CAR|$. All column numbers refer to the
same specification across both panels.

\textbf{Alternative proxies.}
Column~(1) repeats the baseline. Column~(2) substitutes the quadratic
presidential Esteban-Ray index for the linear polarization measure.
For AbVol, the coefficient remains significant ($\beta = 0.081$, $p < 0.05$);
for $|CAR|$, it is directionally consistent but attenuated
($\beta = 0.003$, $p = 0.124$), reflecting the lower discriminating power
of the quadratic form in less-divided states. Column~(3) replaces the
electoral measure with the DW-NOMINATE state-level cross-party ideological
gap, which captures elite ideological distance rather than electoral
division. This proxy is significant for abnormal volume
($\beta = 0.100$, $p < 0.10$) but not for $|CAR|$
($\beta = 0.002$, $p = 0.522$).\footnote{Pairwise
correlations among the standardized proxies indicate that they capture
distinct dimensions of the latent polarization concept: electoral
polarization and the presidential ER index are highly correlated ($r = 0.87$), but
both are weakly correlated with DW-NOMINATE ($r = 0.10$ and $0.16$,
respectively), county-level polarization ($r = 0.45$ and $0.43$),
and county vote SD ($r = 0.08$ and $0.11$). County vote SD and
DW-NOMINATE share moderate correlation ($r = 0.29$), consistent with
geographic sorting and elite polarization capturing related but distinct
phenomena.}

\textbf{Specification robustness.}
Column~(4) augments the baseline with state fixed effects. The AbVol
coefficient is directionally positive but loses precision
($\beta = 0.066$, $p > 0.10$); the $|CAR|$ coefficient is similar
($\beta = 0.012$, $p = 0.144$). Both nulls are consistent with
within-state temporal variation in partisan polarization being noisier
than cross-state variation: of the 124 state$\times$election-cycle cells
that contain auditor-change events, 52 contain only a single event,
limiting the power of a within-state design. Our identification
therefore relies primarily on cross-state differences in the partisan
environment rather than within-state changes over time.
Columns~(5)--(6) test alternative clustering structures. State-level
clustering (column~5) and two-way clustering by firm and state following
\citet{cameron2011} (column~6) leave both outcomes unchanged and
significant: AbVol at $p < 0.001$ in both cases, $|CAR|$ at $p = 0.028$
under state clustering,
indicating that the baseline inference is not driven by the choice of
clustering level.

\textbf{Geographic scale.}
Column~(7) replaces the state-level measure with a county-level analogue
constructed from the same MIT Election Lab presidential returns, matched
to each firm's headquarters county via Compustat zip codes and the HUD
ZIP-COUNTY crosswalk. For $|CAR|$, the coefficient is $\beta = 0.005$
($p = 0.036$, $N = 659$), virtually identical to the state-level
baseline, indicating that the result is not an artifact of geographic
aggregation. For AbVol, the county-level coefficient is near zero
($\beta = 0.022$, $p > 0.10$). A horse-race specification including both state
and county competitiveness simultaneously (Table~\ref{tab:supplementary},
columns~1--2) suggests that state-level variation dominates for AbVol
($\beta_{\text{state}} = 0.125$, $p = 0.012$;
$\beta_{\text{county}} = -0.030$, $p = 0.535$), while neither measure
dominates for $|CAR|$, where both capture overlapping variation in the
marginal investor's political environment.

\textbf{Small-firm subsample.}
Column~(8) restricts the sample to below-median log-asset firms ($N = 339$).
The AbVol coefficient strengthens ($\beta = 0.137$, $p < 0.05$), consistent
with the local-investor channel: smaller firms have more geographically
concentrated retail investor bases, so headquarters-state polarization is
a better-matched proxy. The $|CAR|$ coefficient is directionally
consistent ($\beta = 0.005$, $p = 0.175$) but imprecise given the smaller
subsample. This test also speaks to the sample-selection concern raised in
Section~\ref{sec:design}: because the unmatched 8-K filers are
predominantly smaller, non-Big-4 firms, the below-median subsample is the
segment of our analysis sample most comparable to the unmatched universe.
The fact that the effect strengthens rather than attenuates in this
subsample suggests that sample selection on CRSP/Compustat coverage does
not drive the baseline result.

\textbf{Electoral polarization versus swing voters.}
A natural concern is that electoral polarization captures moderate swing
voters rather than genuinely polarized blocs \citep{fiorina2005}. A 50-50
state could be evenly split because its voters are centrists who swing
between parties, not because it contains ideologically opposed camps. To
address this distinction, column~(9) replaces the baseline measure with the
population-weighted within-state standard deviation of county-level
Democratic vote shares. This measure captures \emph{geographic partisan
sorting}: a high value indicates a state with both deep-red and deep-blue
counties --- genuine local polarization --- while a low value indicates
counties that are uniformly similar regardless of the statewide margin.
For $|CAR|$, the coefficient is $\beta = 0.005$ ($p = 0.056$), marginally
significant and similar in magnitude to the baseline. For AbVol, the
coefficient is directionally positive ($\beta = 0.066$, $p > 0.10$) but
does not reach significance.
Column~(10) includes both the baseline polarization measure and the
county vote-share SD jointly, along with their interaction; the interaction
is insignificant in both panels ($p > 0.30$), indicating that the two
proxies capture overlapping variation rather than a conditional effect.

\textbf{Summary.} Across both panels, the pattern is consistent with the
theoretical hierarchy: abnormal volume --- the direct disagreement
prediction --- is robust to three conceptually distinct polarization
proxies (electoral polarization, ER index, and DW-NOMINATE), alternative
clustering structures, and the small-firm subsample restriction. Absolute
returns are more fragile across alternative proxies, as expected given that
the link from posterior dispersion to $|CAR|$ depends on additional
market-friction conditions. The proxies that are null for $|CAR|$ (ER
index, DW-NOMINATE) are significant for AbVol, indicating that the absence
of a price effect does not imply the absence of disagreement.

To address concerns about finite-sample inference with $N = 678$, we implement
a permutation (randomization) test for both baseline coefficients. We reshuffle
the \textit{Polarization} variable across observations 5,000 times, holding
all other variables fixed, and re-estimate $\beta$ each time. For AbVol, the
actual coefficient falls at the 99.6th percentile of the null distribution,
yielding a one-sided permutation $p$-value of 0.004. For $|CAR|$, the
permutation $p$-value is 0.029. Both indicate that the baseline results are
not artifacts of finite-sample distributional assumptions.
More broadly, the fact that we detect effects at conventional significance
levels despite the moderate sample size implies that the underlying effect
sizes are economically large --- a point reinforced by the subsample tests,
where splits that reduce $N$ substantially still produce significant
coefficients (e.g., Table~\ref{tab:event_type}, dismissal AbVol with
$N = 421$, $p < 0.01$; Table~\ref{tab:event_type}, non-dismissal $|CAR|$
with $N = 257$, $p < 0.05$).
Table~\ref{tab:permutation} reports the full permutation test results.

The polarization effect is stable across regulatory regimes: interacting
polarization with a post-SOX indicator yields insignificant interactions
for both outcomes ($p > 0.49$), and the coefficient is directionally
positive in both the pre-SOX ($N = 317$) and post-SOX ($N = 361$)
subsamples (Table~\ref{tab:supplementary}, columns~3--8).\footnote{The
SEC's 2004 overhaul of Form~8-K (Release No.~33-8400) accelerated filing
deadlines but did not materially alter the substantive content
requirements for Item~4.01 auditor-change disclosures. Only 40 sample
events occur after the PCAOB's introduction of critical audit matter
(CAM) disclosures in 2019--2020, precluding a well-powered test of
whether richer audit-specific information attenuates
polarization-driven disagreement.}

\textbf{Ideological versus affective polarization.}
The political science literature distinguishes ideological polarization
from affective polarization (out-party animus; \citealp{iyengar2012,
iyengar2019}). To test whether the baseline result reflects the latter,
we augment the specification with the ANES feeling-thermometer partisan
differential interacted with state-level partisan exposure (Appendix
Table~\ref{tab:affective}). The affective interaction is null for both
outcomes ($p > 0.10$), while the ideological polarization coefficient is
unchanged. This is consistent with the mechanism operating through the
cross-sectional partisan composition of the local electorate rather than
through time-varying partisan animus, though we note that the ANES series
is coarse and a state- or investor-level affective measure could yield
different conclusions.

\subsection{Exploratory Cross-Sectional Heterogeneity}
\label{sec:exploratory}

Table~\ref{tab:event_type} splits the sample by event type. The pattern
is mixed: polarization significantly predicts volume for dismissals
($\beta = 0.134$, $p = 0.006$) but not non-dismissals, while the
$|CAR|$ pattern reverses (non-dismissals: $\beta = 0.011$, $p = 0.043$;
dismissals: $\beta = 0.003$, $p = 0.189$). Given the inconsistency
across outcomes, we do not treat these splits as informative about the
interpretation channel.

We also explore whether the polarization effect is stronger when filings
provide less objective guidance, using a text-based filing informativeness
measure. The interaction is statistically insignificant for both outcomes
($p = 0.806$ for AbVol; $p = 0.416$ for $|CAR|$).
Appendix Table~\ref{tab:ambiguity} reports an extended analysis.

\textbf{Filing content and the interpretation channel.}
A more direct test of whether the polarization effect operates through
the interpretation of substantive disclosure content uses within-filing
variation in what the 8-K actually says. We construct an
\texttt{explicit\_cause} indicator equal to one when the filing provides
a causal explanation for the auditor change (e.g., ``due to,'' ``because
of,'' ``decided to dismiss''), as opposed to generic Item~304 boilerplate
(34\% of filings). The interaction between polarization and
\texttt{explicit\_cause} is positive and significant for abnormal volume
($\beta = 0.208$, $p = 0.013$): the polarization effect on volume is
concentrated almost entirely in filings that provide substantive causal
content ($\beta = 0.212$, $p = 0.022$ in the explicit-cause subsample
vs.\ $\beta = 0.037$, $p = 0.482$ in the boilerplate subsample). The
interaction is directionally positive but insignificant for $|CAR|$
($\beta = 0.003$, $p = 0.557$). This pattern is consistent with
disagreement arising when investors must interpret qualitatively rich
filing content, not merely when they observe the fact of an auditor change.

We also test whether the polarization effect is amplified in filings
that disclose formal disagreements between the registrant and the
departing auditor, using a corrected disagreement indicator that
distinguishes affirmative disagreement disclosures from the negated
boilerplate required by Item~304 (4.0\% of sample events). The
interaction is null for both outcomes ($p = 0.689$ for AbVol,
$p = 0.716$ for $|CAR|$), though power is limited with only 27
disagreement events. These tests do not isolate audit credibility as
the specific operative channel; the evidence is more consistent with a
general interpretation-of-substantive-content mechanism than with a
narrowly credibility-specific one.

\textbf{Investor-base political heterogeneity.}
As a partial test of whether the effect operates through the investor
base rather than some other state-level channel, we construct a
firm-event measure of investor-base political heterogeneity using 13F
institutional holdings matched to Federal Election Commission individual
contribution records. Following \citet{wintoki2019}, we identify the
political lean of each 13F filer by matching its name to FEC employer
contribution data using fuzzy string matching; for matched events, the
matched institutional share averages 95\% of reported holdings. We
define $\mathit{investor\_het} = 1 - |\mathit{Dem\,share} -
\mathit{Rep\,share}|$, which is maximized when the institutional
investor base is equally split between Democratic- and
Republican-leaning filers (heterogeneous base) and minimized when it is
politically one-sided. The measure is available for 65 post-2013 events,
the period covered by the SEC's structured 13F data sets.

The coefficient on $\mathit{investor\_het}$ is positive for abnormal
volume ($\beta = 0.317$, $p = 0.190$, $N = 65$): events with more
politically mixed institutional investor bases exhibit larger abnormal
volume. The result is directionally consistent with the state-level
evidence and with the theoretical prediction that disagreement scales
with heterogeneity in investor priors, but is imprecisely estimated
given the limited post-2013 subsample. In the same subsample, the
state-level polarization proxy is flat ($\beta = 0.025$, $p = 0.913$),
reflecting both the small sample and the post-2013 composition of these
events. The correlation between the two measures is near zero
($r = -0.003$), consistent with them capturing distinct dimensions of
political heterogeneity---firm-level investor composition versus
state-level electoral environment. We treat this as suggestive rather
than independently significant.

\subsection{Post-Event Price Persistence}
\label{sec:reversal}

As a descriptive check on whether the event-window effects reflect
short-lived overreaction, we examine post-event returns. We regress
signed market-model CARs over $[+2,+20]$ and $[+2,+60]$ trading days
on polarization, controlling for the event-window return. If the
polarization effect reflects noise trading that washes out, the
coefficient should be negative.

Table~\ref{tab:reversal} reports the results. In all four specifications,
the polarization coefficient is positive --- the opposite sign from
the overreaction prediction --- and not significant at conventional levels
(columns~1--2: $\beta = 0.013$, $p = 0.103$ and $\beta = 0.019$,
$p = 0.199$; columns~3--4: $\beta = 0.014$,
$p = 0.088$ and $\beta = 0.019$, $p = 0.187$). There is no evidence
of post-event price reversal in high-polarization states at either
horizon. The absence of reversal is descriptively consistent with
persistent disagreement rather than transient overreaction, though the
coefficients are imprecisely estimated and we do not interpret them as
strong evidence for any specific persistence mechanism.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We document that auditor-change disclosures elicit stronger
disagreement-sensitive market reactions --- particularly abnormal trading
volume --- for listed firms headquartered in more politically competitive
states. The volume result is robust across alternative polarization
proxies and clustering structures; the absolute return result is
directionally consistent but more fragile, as the disagreement framework
predicts. Placebo tests on the same firms' earnings announcements and
officer-change disclosures indicate that this pattern is specific to
auditor-change disclosures rather than generic corporate news. Within the
auditor-change sample, the effect is concentrated in filings that provide
explicit causal explanations for the change, consistent with disagreement
arising from the interpretation of substantive disclosure content.

Several limitations warrant emphasis. Headquarters-state polarization is
a noisy reduced-form proxy for the political-information environment of
a geographically dispersed investor base. A partial investor-level test
using 13F institutional holdings matched to FEC contribution records
finds a positive but imprecisely estimated association between
firm-level investor-base heterogeneity and abnormal volume in the
post-2013 subsample ($\beta = 0.317$, $p = 0.190$, $N = 65$);
extending this measure to the full sample period requires parsing
individual EDGAR 13F filings not covered by the SEC's structured data
sets. The sample ($N = 678$) limits power for interaction tests,
and with only 27 filings disclosing formal disagreements, we cannot test
whether the effect operates specifically through audit-credibility
beliefs. The analysis is necessarily limited to CRSP/Compustat-covered
firms, so the findings may not generalize to the many micro-cap and OTC
registrants that file Item~4.01 disclosures.

The evidence is consistent with a modest limitation of disclosure-based
regulation: standardized mandatory disclosure may not eliminate
disagreement when the filing is interpretation-intensive and investors
process it through different priors.

\section{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:dispersion} and Additional Theoretical Results}
\label{app:proofs}

\noindent\textbf{Setup.} Let there be two investor groups, $D$ and $R$, with
population shares $\omega_D, \omega_R > 0$ and $\omega_D + \omega_R = 1$. Group $g$ holds prior
$\pi_g \in (0,1)$. The mean prior is $\bar{\pi} = \omega_D \pi_D + \omega_R \pi_R$.
After observing signal $s=1$, group $g$ holds posterior
$\phi(\pi_g)$, where the Bayesian updating function is
\[
    \phi(\pi) = \frac{p_H \pi}{p_H \pi + p_L(1-\pi)},
    \qquad p_H > p_L > 0.
\]
The function $\phi$ is strictly increasing and continuous on $(0,1)$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 1: Variance formula.}
The posterior beliefs across groups follow a two-point distribution placing
mass $\omega_D$ at $\phi(\pi_D)$ and mass $\omega_R$ at $\phi(\pi_R)$. The population
mean posterior is $\bar{\phi} = \omega_D \phi(\pi_D) + \omega_R \phi(\pi_R)$. A
standard calculation gives:
\begin{align*}
    \mathrm{Var}[\phi(\pi_g)]
    &= \omega_D\bigl(\phi(\pi_D) - \bar\phi\bigr)^2
     + \omega_R\bigl(\phi(\pi_R) - \bar\phi\bigr)^2 \\
    &= \omega_D (\omega_R)^2 \bigl(\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R)\bigr)^2
     + \omega_R (\omega_D)^2 \bigl(\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R)\bigr)^2 \\
    &= \omega_D \omega_R (\omega_D + \omega_R) \bigl(\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R)\bigr)^2 \\
    &= \omega_D \omega_R \bigl(\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R)\bigr)^2,
\end{align*}
where the second equality uses $\phi(\pi_D) - \bar\phi = \omega_R(\phi(\pi_D) -
\phi(\pi_R))$ and $\phi(\pi_R) - \bar\phi = -\omega_D(\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R))$,
and the last equality uses $\omega_D + \omega_R = 1$. This establishes the variance
formula in Proposition~\ref{prop:dispersion}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 2: Monotonicity in $|\pi_D - \pi_R|$.}
Without loss of generality, assume $\pi_D > \pi_R$. A mean-preserving
spread that increases $|\pi_D - \pi_R|$ by $\epsilon > 0$ while holding
$\bar{\pi}$ fixed requires:
\[
    \pi_D' = \pi_D + \omega_R \epsilon, \qquad
    \pi_R' = \pi_R - \omega_D \epsilon,
\]
so that $\omega_D \pi_D' + \omega_R \pi_R' = \omega_D \pi_D + \omega_R \pi_R = \bar{\pi}$.
We restrict $\epsilon$ to be small enough that $\pi_D' \in (0,1)$ and
$\pi_R' \in (0,1)$; such $\epsilon > 0$ exists because $\pi_D, \pi_R \in
(0,1)$ strictly.

Since $\phi$ is strictly increasing:
\[
    \phi(\pi_D') = \phi(\pi_D + \omega_R\epsilon) > \phi(\pi_D),
    \qquad
    \phi(\pi_R') = \phi(\pi_R - \omega_D\epsilon) < \phi(\pi_R).
\]
Therefore:
\[
    \phi(\pi_D') - \phi(\pi_R')
    > \phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R) > 0.
\]
Since $\omega_D \omega_R > 0$ is unchanged, we conclude:
\[
    \mathrm{Var}[\phi(\pi_g')]
    = \omega_D \omega_R \bigl(\phi(\pi_D') - \phi(\pi_R')\bigr)^2
    > \omega_D \omega_R \bigl(\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R)\bigr)^2
    = \mathrm{Var}[\phi(\pi_g)]. \qquad \square
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Remark (role of mean-preservation).}
The mean-preserving condition $\omega_D \pi_D' + \omega_R \pi_R' = \bar{\pi}$ is
used to hold the average prior fixed, ensuring that the comparison is
\emph{ceteris paribus}: only dispersion changes, not the level of the aggregate
belief. However, mean-preservation is \emph{sufficient}, not \emph{necessary},
for the variance to increase. The formula $\mathrm{Var}[\phi(\pi_g)] =
\omega_D \omega_R (\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R))^2$ depends only on the gap
$|\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R)|$, which is strictly increasing in $|\pi_D -
\pi_R|$ for any $\pi_D, \pi_R \in (0,1)$, regardless of what happens to the
mean. If affective polarization causes an asymmetric shift --- one group's prior
moves while the other's stays fixed --- the mean shifts too, but the posterior
variance still increases as long as the gap $|\pi_D - \pi_R|$ widens. The
mean-preserving restriction serves to isolate the dispersion effect from a
change in average pessimism or optimism, not to make the monotonicity result
logically possible.

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Proposition A1: Unified Channel Representation.}

\medskip
\noindent Let $\sigma(x) \equiv e^x/(1+e^x)$ denote the logistic map, with
$\sigma'(x) = \sigma(x)(1-\sigma(x)) > 0$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$.
For any prior $\pi \in (0,1)$, define prior log-odds $a(\pi) \equiv
\log[\pi/(1-\pi)]$ and objective log-likelihood ratio $\ell \equiv
\log(p_H/p_L) > 0$.

\begin{proposition}[Unified channel]
\label{prop:unified}
\label{prop:trust}
\label{prop:precision}
Let each group have posterior $\mu_g = \sigma(X_g)$, where the latent
log-odds index $X_g = a(\pi_g) + \tau_g \kappa_g \ell$ may differ across
groups through any combination of heterogeneous priors $\pi_g$, trust
$\tau_g \in [0,1]$, or perceived signal precision $\kappa_g > 0$.
When $\tau_g = \kappa_g = 1$ for all groups, $\sigma(X_g) = \phi(\pi_g)$,
the Bayesian posterior of Equation~\eqref{eq:bayes}, so the main model is
a special case. Then:
\[
    \mathrm{Var}(\mu_g) = \omega_D \omega_R \bigl[\sigma(X_D) - \sigma(X_R)\bigr]^2,
\]
and any mean-preserving spread of the latent indices --- increasing
$|X_D - X_R|$ while holding $\omega_D X_D + \omega_R X_R$ fixed --- strictly
increases $\mathrm{Var}(\mu_g)$.
In particular, if polarization widens the cross-group gap in any one of the
three underlying components via a mean-preserving spread --- prior log-odds,
trust, or perceived precision --- posterior variance and disagreement-sensitive
trading outcomes strictly increase.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The variance formula is the two-point distribution result (Step~1 above).
For the comparative static, consider $X_D' = X_D + \omega_R\epsilon$,
$X_R' = X_R - \omega_D\epsilon$ for $\epsilon > 0$, which preserves the
weighted mean. Since $\sigma$ is strictly increasing,
$\sigma(X_D') > \sigma(X_D)$ and $\sigma(X_R') < \sigma(X_R)$, so
$|\sigma(X_D')-\sigma(X_R')| > |\sigma(X_D)-\sigma(X_R)|$ and the variance
strictly increases. The same argument applies when the source of
heterogeneity is trust alone ($\pi$ and $\kappa$ common), precision alone
($\pi$ and $\tau$ common), or any combination --- in each case, the relevant
parameter enters $X_g$ monotonically, and a mean-preserving spread widens
the gap. $\square$
\end{proof}
