\begin{abstract}
This paper studies whether political polarization changes how investors interpret
standardized auditor disclosures. We focus on auditor changes disclosed under
Item 4.01 of Form 8-K, a setting in which all investors observe the same
mandated public signal at the same time. We argue that polarization matters not
because it alters the disclosure itself, but because it increases heterogeneity
in investors' trust in auditors and in the regulatory institutions that make
those disclosures credible. In a Bayesian framework with heterogeneous priors,
polarization widens posterior-belief dispersion after a common audit signal,
thereby increasing disagreement-driven trading volume and the magnitude of price
responses. The effect is strongest when the disclosure is more ambiguous, so
that priors matter more relative to signal precision. Empirically, we test
whether market reactions to auditor-change disclosures are stronger in more
polarized political environments and whether the effect is concentrated in
ambiguity-rich Item 4.01 filings. The paper's contribution is deliberately
narrow: rather than claiming that polarization affects financial disagreement in
general, we provide audit-specific evidence that polarization changes the
interpretation of auditor-originated public signals in a highly regulated
setting. This framing improves the paper's novelty relative to the broader
polarization-in-finance literature and sharpens its fit with auditing and
capital-markets research.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Auditor-related disclosures are intended to provide a standardized,
institution-backed signal about the reliability of firms' financial reporting.
Yet market reactions to those disclosures vary substantially across otherwise
similar events. This paper argues that one important source of that variation is
political polarization. Specifically, we propose that polarization increases the
cross-sectional dispersion of investor priors about the credibility of auditors,
audit oversight, and the institutions that support financial reporting.
Consequently, even when investors observe the same public audit signal at the
same time, they interpret that signal less uniformly in more polarized
environments.

We study auditor changes disclosed under Item 4.01 of Form 8-K. This setting is
well suited to our question for four reasons. First, the disclosure is
mandatory, highly standardized, and publicly released to all investors
simultaneously. That sharply limits explanations based on staggered information
arrival or selective disclosure. Second, auditor changes are often intrinsically
ambiguous. A switch can reflect ordinary matching between the client and a new
auditor, but it can also reflect disagreements over accounting issues, internal
control concerns, auditor-client conflict, or deteriorating reporting quality
\citep{johnson1990,hossain2014}. Third, the economic meaning of the disclosure
is institution-dependent. Investors must evaluate the credibility of the outgoing
and incoming auditors, the reliability of management's stated reasons for the
change, and the strength of the regulatory framework surrounding the filing.
Fourth, the item is central to the concerns of auditing scholars and policy
makers because it is one of the cleanest mandated disclosures through which
investors learn about auditor turnover.

The paper's central thesis is narrower than the broad claim that ``polarization
affects markets.'' A growing literature already shows that political polarization
affects financial decisions, expectations, and disagreement more generally
\citep{kempf2024,hao2026}. Overclaiming novelty against that literature would be
unwise. Our contribution is instead audit-specific: we study whether political
polarization changes the \emph{interpretation of auditor-originated public
signals} in a setting with standardized disclosure timing and standardized
minimum content. The paper therefore fits naturally at the intersection of
archival auditing, capital markets, and political economy, while maintaining a
novel angle that is not simply a relabeling of existing polarization research.
Just as importantly, the paper now ties its empirical proxies explicitly to the
polarization-measurement literature: DW--NOMINATE is used as a national elite
polarization benchmark, while Shor--McCarty state legislative polarization is
used as the cross-sectional local-environment measure.

The theoretical mechanism is straightforward. Investors observe the same public
audit signal but differ in their priors about how credible and informative the
signal is. We interpret those prior differences as arising from heterogeneous
institutional trust: trust in auditors as independent monitors, trust in the
firm's disclosure about the change, and trust in the audit-regulatory regime
that governs the event. Political polarization increases the dispersion of those
priors. In a Bayesian environment, a mean-preserving spread in priors yields a
wider dispersion of posterior beliefs after the same public disclosure. Under
standard heterogeneous-belief trading logic, greater posterior dispersion should
translate into more disagreement-driven trading volume and larger \emph{absolute}
price responses \citep{harris1993,kandel1995,hong2007}. The magnitude effect
should be strongest when the filing is more ambiguous, because investors then
place more weight on priors and less weight on the signal itself.

This mechanism leads to a more defensible empirical thesis than the one in the
earliest version of the project. The paper should \emph{not} be framed around a
universal signed-return prediction for all auditor changes. Some changes are
plausibly favorable, some unfavorable, and many are difficult to classify
cleanly ex ante. The strongest predictions concern disagreement-oriented
outcomes: abnormal trading volume and the absolute value of cumulative abnormal
returns around the filing. Signed-return tests can still be informative, but
only in clearly directional subsamples such as Big-4 downgrades, disclosed
disagreements, or events with explicit negative language.

The paper's contribution is threefold. First, it contributes to the auditing and
capital-markets literature by showing that the informativeness of auditor-change
disclosures depends not only on the disclosure content itself, but also on the
political environment in which investors receive the disclosure. Second, it
contributes to the polarization-in-finance literature by identifying an
institutional-trust channel in a setting where the public signal is standardized
and mandated, rather than generated by partisan media, analysts, or firm
managers. Third, it contributes to the heterogeneous-beliefs literature by
bringing that framework to an audit-specific setting in which disagreement can be
observed through event-window volume and absolute returns.

The paper also has regulatory relevance. Disclosure-based audit regulation often
implicitly assumes that more standardized auditor communication should help
investors form more accurate and more common beliefs. Our analysis suggests a
limit to that logic. When investors differ sharply in their priors about
institutional credibility, standardized disclosure may not resolve disagreement.
Indeed, when the disclosure is ambiguous, it may magnify disagreement by giving
investors more material to interpret through divergent priors. In that sense,
the effectiveness of disclosure-based audit regulation depends partly on the
political and institutional environment in which the disclosure is received.

\section{Related Literature}
\label{sec:lit}

\subsection{Audit Signals in Capital Markets}

A long literature shows that auditor-originated disclosures matter for investors.
Early studies document market reactions to audit qualifications and other audit
report signals \citep{firth1978,dodd1984}. Broad review work then establishes
that audit quality and auditor reputation are linked to capital-market outcomes
more generally \citep{francis2004,defond2014}. More recent research on expanded
audit reporting shows that richer auditor communication can affect users of
financial reports and related outcomes \citep{gutierrez2018,burke2023}. This
literature establishes the economic relevance of audit signals, but it largely
leaves open an important question: why do otherwise similar standardized auditor
disclosures generate markedly different reaction magnitudes across firms and
environments?

Our paper addresses that question by shifting attention from the average
informativeness of auditor disclosures to the \emph{heterogeneity in their
interpretation}. This distinction is important. If the same standardized
disclosure is processed differently in more versus less polarized environments,
then the market consequences of audit disclosure cannot be understood by studying
signal content alone.

\subsection{Auditor Changes as a Disclosure Setting}

Auditor changes are among the cleanest settings in which to study the market
interpretation of audit-related information. Item 4.01 filings must be made
promptly, follow a relatively standardized structure, and often disclose whether
the registrant and former auditor disagreed on accounting or auditing matters.
Classic work studies auditor switches as part of the market for audit services
\citep{johnson1990}. More directly related evidence shows that managers'
voluntary disclosure of reasons for auditor changes affects the capital-market
reaction to those events \citep{hossain2014}. The broad lesson is that the
content and context of auditor changes matter for investors.

We build on that literature but shift the emphasis from event content alone to
the political environment in which the event is interpreted. Our question is not
merely whether some auditor changes are worse than others. It is whether
\emph{similarly coded auditor-change events} can generate different reaction
magnitudes because polarization changes how investors process the same public
signal.

\subsection{Political Economy of Auditing and Regulation}

A related literature shows that accounting, auditing, and financial reporting are
embedded in political institutions. Recent work demonstrates that political
polarization can disrupt SEC oversight and generate real market consequences
\citep{bens2026}. Other work shows that political ideology can shape reporting
regulation itself, including SEC commissioners' views on IFRS adoption
\citep{becker2025}. On the auditing side, auditors appear to respond to firm-
level political risk through higher fees, longer lags, and a greater likelihood
of going-concern reporting \citep{chen2024}. These studies are highly relevant
because they establish that auditing and reporting do not operate outside the
political sphere.

However, most of that literature focuses on the \emph{supply side} of auditing,
regulation, or enforcement. Our paper instead studies the \emph{demand side}: how
investors interpret an audit-related disclosure once it is released. That
mechanism is distinct from auditor effort, regulator ideology, or political risk
inside the client firm. It is also the mechanism that best preserves novelty for
this paper.

\subsection{Political Polarization in Finance}

A growing finance literature shows that political polarization affects financial
decisions and beliefs. \citet{kempf2024} review evidence that partisan and
ideological divisions shape the behavior of households, executives, and
financial intermediaries. Most closely related to our project, \citet{hao2026}
show that political polarization is associated with greater analyst disagreement.
That paper is important for two reasons. First, it strongly supports our premise
that polarization can widen disagreement among sophisticated market participants.
Second, it means this paper should not claim to be the first to connect
polarization to financial disagreement in general.

Our contribution is therefore deliberately narrower and more audit-specific. We
examine whether polarization changes the interpretation of \emph{auditor-originated
public signals} in a setting with standardized timing, mandated content, and
clear regulatory salience. This positioning differentiates the project from work
on analyst disagreement, politically slanted media, or partisan differences in
general financial expectations.

\subsection{Heterogeneous Beliefs, Common Signals, and Disagreement}

Our theoretical mechanism draws on the heterogeneous-beliefs literature.
Differences of opinion can generate trading even in the absence of differential
private information \citep{harris1993}. When agents begin from different priors,
identical public signals need not produce identical posteriors \citep{kandel1995}.
Broader work links disagreement to trading volume, volatility, and asset prices
\citep{hong2007}. Two recent papers are especially useful for our setting.
\citet{armstrong2024} show that beliefs can diverge after the same financial
information when agents are uncertain about signal precision, and
\citet{huang2024} show that disagreement about public information quality can
shape price efficiency and trading around public news.

This literature provides the bridge to our auditing setting. Item 4.01 filings
are common public signals. Political polarization creates a plausible source of
heterogeneity in priors about how credible and informative those signals are.
The combination implies a clear prediction: polarization should amplify
market-based measures of disagreement, especially when the disclosure is more
ambiguous and therefore less capable of overwhelming prior differences.

\section{Theory}
\label{sec:theory}

We develop a simple framework that formalizes how political polarization can
change market reactions to auditor-change disclosures. The model is intentionally
parsimonious. Its purpose is not to explain every determinant of price formation
around auditor changes. Rather, it isolates the paper's novel mechanism: greater
polarization increases heterogeneity in priors about the credibility and meaning
of a common auditor signal, thereby amplifying disagreement after the filing.

\subsection{Setting}

Let $\theta \in \{0,1\}$ denote the latent state associated with an
Item 4.01 event, where $\theta=1$ represents a relatively favorable underlying
state and $\theta=0$ an unfavorable one. The filing conveys a public signal
$s \in \{g,b\}$, where $g$ is more favorable than $b$. In the empirical setting,
$s$ should be interpreted broadly: it summarizes the bundle of facts in the
filing, including whether the change followed a resignation or dismissal,
whether disagreements or reportable events were disclosed, and whether the new
auditor appears higher or lower quality than the predecessor.

Conditional on the latent state, signal precision is
\begin{align}
    \Pr(s=g\mid \theta=1) &= p_H, \\
    \Pr(s=g\mid \theta=0) &= p_L,
\end{align}
with $p_H > p_L$. The informativeness of the filing is governed by the gap
$p_H-p_L$. Smaller values correspond to more ambiguous disclosures.

A unit mass of investors observes the same filing at the same time. Investors
differ only in their priors. Investor $i$ assigns prior probability
$\pi_i \in (0,1)$ to the favorable state. We interpret variation in $\pi_i$ as
arising from heterogeneous institutional trust: trust in the outgoing and incoming
auditors, trust in the firm's disclosure about the switch, and trust in the
audit-regulatory institutions that make the filing credible. Political
polarization widens the cross-sectional dispersion of these priors.

\subsection{Polarization as Prior Dispersion}

Let the average prior remain fixed at $\bar{\pi}$, but let higher polarization
induce a mean-preserving spread in the distribution of $\pi_i$. This assumption
is essential for the empirical interpretation. The mechanism does \emph{not}
require polarization to make investors uniformly more optimistic or more
pessimistic about audit signals. It requires only that polarization makes
investors less likely to begin from a common prior. That implication is exactly
why abnormal volume and absolute returns are the paper's natural primary
outcomes.

\subsection{Belief Updating}

If the public signal is favorable ($s=g$), investor $i$'s posterior belief is
\begin{equation}
    \mu_i(g)
    = \Pr(\theta=1\mid s=g)
    = \frac{p_H\pi_i}{p_H\pi_i + p_L(1-\pi_i)}.
\end{equation}
If the signal is unfavorable ($s=b$), the posterior is
\begin{equation}
    \mu_i(b)
    = \Pr(\theta=1\mid s=b)
    = \frac{(1-p_H)\pi_i}{(1-p_H)\pi_i + (1-p_L)(1-\pi_i)}.
\end{equation}
In both cases, the posterior is monotone in the prior. A wider cross-sectional
dispersion of priors therefore translates into a wider dispersion of posteriors
after the same public filing.

\begin{proposition}[Polarization increases posterior-belief dispersion]
\label{prop:dispersion}
Suppose the distribution of priors under high polarization is a mean-preserving
spread of the distribution of priors under low polarization. Then, for either
signal realization $s \in \{g,b\}$, the cross-sectional variance of posterior
beliefs is weakly higher under high polarization.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For either signal realization, the posterior is a monotone transformation of the
prior. A mean-preserving spread in priors therefore weakly increases the
cross-sectional spread of posteriors. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Prices, Trading, and Absolute Reactions}

Let investor demand be increasing in the difference between the investor's
posterior valuation and the market-clearing price. In standard
heterogeneous-belief settings, greater dispersion in posterior beliefs increases
the willingness of investors to trade against one another, generating higher
trading volume \citep{harris1993,kandel1995}. When arbitrage is imperfect because
of risk-bearing or short-sale constraints, greater dispersion in posteriors also
increases the magnitude of the equilibrium price response to the public signal.
These implications motivate our empirical focus on two disagreement-oriented
outcomes: abnormal trading volume and the absolute value of abnormal returns.

\begin{proposition}[Polarization amplifies disagreement-oriented market outcomes]
\label{prop:market}
Holding constant the content of the auditor-change disclosure, higher political
polarization increases abnormal trading volume and the magnitude of the price
reaction to the disclosure.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Signal Ambiguity}

The role of polarization should depend on how informative the filing is. If
$p_H-p_L$ is large, the signal is precise and investors' posteriors depend less
on their priors. If $p_H-p_L$ is small, the signal is ambiguous and priors matter
more.

\begin{proposition}[Ambiguity strengthens the polarization effect]
\label{prop:ambiguity}
The effect of prior dispersion on posterior-belief dispersion is stronger when
signal precision is lower. Consequently, the effect of political polarization on
abnormal volume and absolute returns is stronger for more ambiguous
Item 4.01 disclosures.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
As signal precision falls, posterior beliefs place greater weight on priors.
Therefore, a given difference in priors translates into a larger difference in
posteriors. The result follows directly from the Bayes expressions above. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Signed reactions belong in directional subsamples]
\label{cor:signed}
A common signed-return prediction does not generally follow from the model when
Item 4.01 events pool favorable, unfavorable, and ambiguous auditor changes.
Signed-return tests are informative only in subsamples where the filing's
average directional implication is sufficiently clear.
\end{corollary}

The corollary is important for positioning the paper. The earliest thesis of the
project risked overreaching by implying a universal signed effect. The theory
here shows why the cleaner and more novel contribution lies in disagreement-type
outcomes and in the ambiguity interaction.

\section{Hypothesis Development}
\label{sec:hypotheses}

The theory suggests that polarization should increase disagreement-based market
responses to auditor-change disclosures. We therefore focus the main hypotheses
on abnormal volume and absolute returns rather than signed reactions.

\begin{hyp}
\label{h1}
Abnormal trading volume around auditor-change disclosures is higher in more
politically polarized environments.
\end{hyp}

\begin{hyp}
\label{h2}
The absolute cumulative abnormal return around an auditor-change disclosure is
higher in more politically polarized environments.
\end{hyp}

\begin{hyp}
\label{h3}
The positive relation between political polarization and disagreement-oriented
market reactions to auditor-change disclosures is stronger when the disclosure is
more ambiguous.
\end{hyp}

\begin{hyp}
\label{h4}
The relation between polarization and disagreement-oriented market reactions is
stronger when the audit-institution channel is more salient, such as in events
involving disclosed disagreements, dismissals, or auditor-quality downgrades.
\end{hyp}

Hypothesis~\ref{h4} is not intended to replace the baseline design. Rather, it
provides a mechanism-oriented refinement that improves the paper's contribution
relative to a generic story that stocks in polarized states simply react more to
all types of news.

\section{Empirical Design}
\label{sec:design}

\subsection{Sample Construction}

\textbf{Auditor-change events.} We identify auditor changes using Form 8-K
filings disclosing a change in the registrant's certifying accountant under Item
4.01 for 2001--2023. We retain original 8-K filings that can be matched to CRSP,
Compustat, and Audit Analytics, and we require sufficient return and trading
volume data to estimate event-window outcomes. We exclude observations with
confounding major announcements on the same day when those announcements would
make attribution of the market reaction implausible. The final sample consists of
[PLACEHOLDER: N] auditor-change events for [PLACEHOLDER: N] unique firms.

\textbf{Event coding.} A major upgrade relative to the prior draft is to code the
filings at the event level rather than rely on a single coarse switch indicator.
At a minimum, the empirical file should distinguish (i) resignation versus
dismissal versus non-reappointment, (ii) disclosed disagreement or reportable
event, (iii) movement toward or away from the Big 4, and (iv) whether the filing
contains a concrete explanation for the change. These dimensions are central to
the theory because they govern directional clarity and ambiguity.

\subsection{Dependent Variables}

\textbf{Absolute cumulative abnormal return ($|CAR|$).} We estimate abnormal
returns using a standard market model over the estimation window
$[-252,-46]$ relative to the filing date. The main return outcome is
$|CAR(-1,+1)|$, which captures the magnitude of the market reaction while
remaining agnostic about sign. Robustness tests should consider
$|CAR(0,+1)|$ and $|CAR(-1,0)|$ as well as market-adjusted and Fama--French style
benchmarks.

\textbf{Abnormal trading volume ($AbVol$).} We measure abnormal volume as the
event-window average log turnover minus the firm's mean log turnover over a
pre-event benchmark window such as $[-60,-11]$. This variable should be treated
as a co-equal primary outcome because it maps most directly to the paper's
disagreement mechanism.

\textbf{Supplementary disagreement outcomes.} A stronger version of the final
paper should add one or more supplementary proxies: abnormal bid-ask spreads,
intraday realized volatility, or abnormal message-board/news dispersion when
feasible. These are not necessary for the core contribution, but they would make
the disagreement interpretation more convincing.

\subsection{Political Polarization Measures}

The main explanatory variable should be a local political-polarization measure
linked to firm headquarters. The current draft's idea of using a polarization
measure derived from U.S. House election returns is sensible, but the paper will
be stronger if it presents the variable clearly and motivates why headquarters
location is relevant.

A transparent implementation is to define
\begin{equation}
\textit{Polarization}_{s,t}
= 1 - \sum_{p \in \{D,R,\text{Other}\}} \left( \textit{VoteShare}_{p,s,t} \right)^2,
\end{equation}
or, if available, to use an Esteban--Ray style polarization metric. Either way,
the paper should present the variable as a proxy for the extent to which the
firm's local political environment fosters divergence in institutional trust and
interpretive priors.

The paper should also report at least two alternative specifications in
robustness tests: (i) a simpler electoral closeness measure such as one minus the
absolute Democrat--Republican vote-share gap, and (ii) a state-legislator or
elite-ideology polarization measure if available. Using multiple measures will
help show that the main inferences do not depend on one particular electoral
formula.

\subsection{Ambiguity Construction}

A major revision is to make ambiguity explicit and measurable. I recommend an
event-level ambiguity index built from the filing text and observable switch
characteristics. Define indicators for the following features:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=0.25em]
    \item \textbf{No explicit directional cue:} the filing does not disclose a
    disagreement, reportable event, or other clearly negative signal.
    \item \textbf{Same-tier switch:} the change is not an obvious upgrade or
    downgrade in auditor quality (for example, Big-4 to non-Big-4, non-Big-4 to
    Big-4, or movement across a pre-specified quality tier).
    \item \textbf{Sparse explanation:} the filing uses boilerplate or offers
    limited firm-specific explanation for the change.
    \item \textbf{No clear triggering event:} the filing does not tie the change
    to a restatement, internal control problem, merger, bankruptcy, or other
    major corporate event.
\end{enumerate}
Let $\textit{AmbiguityIndex}_i$ be the standardized sum of these indicators.
Higher values correspond to more ambiguity. The advantage of this index is that
it makes the theory operational: polarization should matter most exactly where
directional clarity is weakest.

The paper should still report single-proxy versions of ambiguity for
interpretability, but the composite index ought to be the main specification.

\subsection{Baseline Specification}

The core event-level specification is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:main}
Y_i = \alpha + \beta_1 \, \textit{Polarization}_{s(i),t(i)}
+ \beta_2 \, \textit{AmbiguityIndex}_i
+ \beta_3 \, \textit{Polarization}_{s(i),t(i)} \times \textit{AmbiguityIndex}_i
+ \gamma'X_{i,t(i)-1} + \tau_{t(i)} + \delta_{j(i)} + \varepsilon_i,
\end{equation}
where $Y_i$ is either $|CAR|$ or $AbVol$, $\tau_{t(i)}$ are year fixed effects,
$\delta_{j(i)}$ are industry fixed effects, and $X_{i,t(i)-1}$ includes standard
firm controls: size, market-to-book, leverage, return on assets, cash holdings,
prior stock volatility, prior turnover, analyst coverage, institutional
ownership, indicators for recent losses or financial distress, and a control for
prior restatement or internal control weakness when available.

The coefficient of first-order interest is $\beta_3$. A positive estimate would
show that polarization matters most exactly when the filing is harder to
interpret on its face. That interaction is the most direct empirical mapping of
the theory and should be the centerpiece of the design rather than a late-stage
robustness test.

\subsection{Directional and Mechanism Specifications}

The paper should add two related but secondary specifications.

\textbf{Directional subsamples.} Estimate signed-CAR regressions within event
categories whose average directional implication is relatively clear. Examples
include disclosed disagreements, dismissals accompanied by negative language, or
Big-4 downgrades. These tests help show that the paper does not ignore sign;
rather, it studies sign only where theory says doing so is sensible.

\textbf{Audit-institution salience.} To test Hypothesis~\ref{h4}, interact
polarization with indicators for disclosed disagreements, dismissals,
reportable events, or quality-sensitive auditor changes. If the mechanism truly
runs through audit-institution trust, the effect of polarization should be most
pronounced when the filing foregrounds auditor credibility and conflict.

\subsection{Identification and Threats to Inference}

The central empirical challenge is not simple measurement error in polarization.
It is whether polarization is proxying for other local characteristics that also
affect market reactions. The draft should therefore confront the following issues
explicitly.

\textbf{Local information environments.} More polarized states may differ in
media structure, investor clientele, or local sophistication. The paper should
control for analyst coverage, institutional ownership, local income and
education, and pre-event return volatility. Results should also be checked within
subsamples of firms with richer information environments.

\textbf{Selection into auditor changes.} Polarization may affect which firms
experience auditor changes or what types of changes occur. That concern does not
invalidate the event-study design, but it does mean the interpretation is
conditional on an auditor-change event having occurred. A helpful appendix table
would report whether polarization predicts the incidence or type of auditor
change in the broader firm-year panel.

\textbf{National political shocks.} Because polarization trends upward over time,
year fixed effects are necessary but may not be sufficient. The paper should show
that the results are not driven entirely by election years, the post-2016
period, or a small number of nationally salient episodes.

\textbf{Generic volatility story.} The most serious threat to the contribution is
a reduced-form interpretation that stocks headquartered in polarized states have
higher event sensitivity for all reasons. To address this, the paper should
include placebo tests using other standardized 8-K items that are economically
important but less tied to auditor credibility. If polarization matters much more
for Item 4.01 than for those placebo events, the audit-specific mechanism is far
more credible.

\textbf{Institutional-trust channel.} The ideal version of the paper would add at
least one direct proxy for the trust mechanism. Candidate moderators include
local survey-based trust in institutions, auditor reputation salience, firm-level
governance quality, or filing text that explicitly invokes disagreements or
reportable events. These tests need not be perfect; even imperfect mechanism
evidence would materially strengthen the paper.

\subsection{Recommended Tables and Figures}

The final paper should be organized around a clear table architecture:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=0.25em]
    \item \textbf{Table 1:} Sample construction and descriptive statistics.
    \item \textbf{Table 2:} Baseline regressions for $AbVol$ and $|CAR|$.
    \item \textbf{Table 3:} Ambiguity-interaction tests.
    \item \textbf{Table 4:} Audit-institution salience and directional subsamples.
    \item \textbf{Table 5:} Robustness and placebo tests.
    \item \textbf{Figure 1:} Event-timing illustration and coding examples.
    \item \textbf{Figure 2:} Nonparametric relation between polarization and event
    reactions, separately for low- and high-ambiguity filings.
\end{enumerate}

This architecture will help the paper read like a coherent archival study rather
than a broad conceptual proposal.

\section{Results and Draft Publication-Style Write-up}
\label{sec:results}

Because the paper is still in a pre-estimation stage, this section is written as
a publication-oriented template for the eventual results discussion. The goal is
not to fabricate evidence, but to ensure that once estimates are available the
paper can be written in a tight top-journal style with the contribution already
well defined.

\subsection{Descriptive Evidence}

Table~1 should summarize the sample by event type, ambiguity, and polarization.
The text should first establish that Item 4.01 filings are heterogeneous but not
idiosyncratic. A useful opening paragraph for the eventual results section is:

\begin{quote}
``The sample contains substantial heterogeneity in the content of auditor-change
filings. A nontrivial fraction of events disclose disagreements or reportable
events, while many filings provide only limited firm-specific explanation for the
change. This heterogeneity is important because the paper's mechanism predicts
that polarization matters most when the filing leaves more room for
interpretation. Descriptive evidence is consistent with that premise: both
abnormal trading volume and the absolute value of event-window returns are larger
for more ambiguous filings, particularly in firms headquartered in more
polarized states.''
\end{quote}

The final paper should include at least one simple plot of mean $AbVol$ and mean
$|CAR|$ across polarization terciles, separately for low- and high-ambiguity
events. That figure will offer an intuitive first look before the regressions.

\subsection{Baseline Tests}

Table~2 should estimate Equation~\eqref{eq:main} first without and then with the
ambiguity interaction. The paper should treat $AbVol$ and $|CAR|$ as parallel
primary dependent variables.

Suggested write-up language:

\begin{quote}
``The baseline results indicate that auditor-change disclosures generate stronger
disagreement-oriented market responses in more polarized environments. The
coefficient on \textit{Polarization} is positive in both the abnormal-volume and
absolute-return specifications, consistent with the prediction that investors are
less likely to interpret a common auditor disclosure uniformly when the local
political environment is more polarized. Economically, the estimates imply that a
one-standard-deviation increase in polarization is associated with an increase of
[PLACEHOLDER]\% in abnormal trading volume and [PLACEHOLDER] basis points in the
absolute event-window abnormal return.''
\end{quote}

The final text should emphasize economic magnitude, not just statistical
significance. It should also explicitly state that these results concern reaction
\emph{magnitude}, not a universal signed effect.

\subsection{Ambiguity as the Central Test}

Table~3 should present the interaction between polarization and the ambiguity
index. This is the paper's key test.

Suggested write-up language:

\begin{quote}
``The interaction between \textit{Polarization} and
\textit{AmbiguityIndex} is positive and statistically significant in both the
abnormal-volume and absolute-return regressions. This finding is central to the
paper's contribution. It indicates that polarization matters most exactly when
the Item 4.01 filing provides weaker directional guidance and leaves more room
for interpretation. The result is therefore difficult to reconcile with a purely
generic volatility story and instead supports the paper's trust-based
heterogeneous-priors mechanism.''
\end{quote}

The paper should then unpack the ambiguity index into its components. Showing
that the result is particularly strong for sparse explanations and same-tier
switches would be especially persuasive because those are cases where the filing
itself gives investors less help in classifying the event.

\subsection{Mechanism and Directional Subsamples}

Table~4 should present mechanism-oriented analyses. The strongest tests will show
larger effects when the audit-institution channel is most salient.

Suggested write-up language:

\begin{quote}
``The effect of polarization is more pronounced in events that foreground auditor
credibility and conflict, including dismissals, filings that disclose a
reportable event or disagreement, and changes that represent a plausible decline
in auditor quality. These patterns are consistent with the idea that
polarization amplifies disagreement by increasing heterogeneity in trust toward
auditors and the institutions governing the disclosure. By contrast, in clearly
routine or low-salience auditor changes, the relation between polarization and
reaction magnitude is materially weaker.''
\end{quote}

Directional signed-return tests should appear only after the disagreement-based
results are established. For example, the paper can examine whether negative-
content subsamples show more negative signed CARs in polarized environments.
Framed this way, signed tests support the mechanism without becoming the core
contribution.

\subsection{Robustness and Falsification}

Table~5 should stress falsification and credibility.

Suggested write-up language:

\begin{quote}
``The main inferences are robust to alternative event windows, alternative
abnormal-return models, alternative polarization measures, and the exclusion of
election years and the post-2016 period. Placebo tests further show that the
relation between polarization and reaction magnitude is substantially weaker for
other standardized 8-K events that are economically important but less tied to
auditor credibility. Together, these results support an audit-specific
interpretation rather than a generalized tendency for firms in polarized states
to exhibit stronger event sensitivity.''
\end{quote}

The placebo design is especially important for novelty. Without it, the paper may
look like a broad political-finance event-study with an auditor-change
application. With it, the paper becomes much more clearly an auditing paper.

\subsection{What the Final Results Section Must Avoid}

The final write-up should avoid four common mistakes.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=0.25em]
    \item Do not state or imply that the paper identifies the first relation
    between polarization and financial disagreement. That claim is no longer
    tenable given the nearby literature.
    \item Do not overemphasize signed average returns across all auditor changes.
    That framing is inconsistent with the theory and will invite easy criticism.
    \item Do not treat the ambiguity test as a peripheral robustness exercise.
    It is the most direct test of the paper's mechanism.
    \item Do not rely solely on a headquarters-state polarization proxy without
    discussing why it is relevant and what its limitations are.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper argues that political polarization changes how investors interpret
standardized auditor disclosures. The contribution is not a sweeping claim that
politics matters for financial markets in every setting, nor a generic extension
of the idea that polarization widens disagreement. Instead, the paper identifies
an audit-specific mechanism: polarization increases heterogeneity in
institutional trust and therefore in prior beliefs about the meaning of
\emph{auditor-originated public signals}. In a setting where all investors
observe the same mandated disclosure, that mechanism predicts more
disagreement-driven trade and larger absolute price responses, especially when
the filing is ambiguous.

This reframing materially improves the paper's novelty and fit. The project now
speaks directly to auditing and capital-markets audiences because it uses a core
auditor disclosure, a mechanism grounded in the institutional role of auditing,
and predictions that map cleanly to event-study outcomes. It also aligns better
with the adjacent polarization literature by making a narrower and more credible
claim.

For a conference and journal submission, the next empirical priorities are clear:
(i) finish event-level coding of directional clarity and ambiguity,
(ii) make the ambiguity interaction central in the baseline design,
(iii) add at least one mechanism-oriented test of the institutional-trust
channel, and (iv) use placebo 8-K events to demonstrate that the effect is more
audit-specific than a generic political-volatility story. If those steps are
executed well, the project can be positioned as a credible archival auditing
paper with a novel political-economy mechanism rather than as a broad political
finance paper with an auditing application.
