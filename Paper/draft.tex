% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{abstract}
We examine whether political polarization affects how investors interpret
auditor-related information. We argue that polarization increases heterogeneity in investors’ priors, institutional trust, and perceived signal precision, causing the same auditor disclosure to generate more dispersed interpretations. Using auditor change events disclosed in Form 8-K (Item 4.01), we
show that polarization amplifies absolute abnormal returns and abnormal trading
volume around these events. Effects are stronger for more ambiguous signals,
consistent with the idea that polarization matters most when a disclosure is
harder to map into a clear valuation implication. These findings contribute to
the auditing, capital markets, and political economy literatures, and have
implications for disclosure-based audit regulation.
\end{abstract}

% ── 1. Introduction ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A foundational assumption in capital markets research is that investors interpret
public disclosures in a broadly consistent manner. Audit reports, in particular,
are designed to convey a standardized, institution-backed signal about the
reliability of financial statements. Yet the premise of homogeneous interpretation
sits uneasily alongside a growing body of evidence that political polarization
fundamentally reshapes how individuals process information, form beliefs, and
respond to institutions \citep{kempf2024, bordalo2023}. This paper asks whether
political polarization affects how investors interpret auditor-related information
--- and finds that it does, in economically meaningful ways.

We study auditor changes disclosed in Form 8-K (Item 4.01). These events are
well-suited to our question for three reasons. First, they are public, standardized
disclosures that all investors observe simultaneously, so differences in market
reactions are more likely to reflect differences in interpretation than
differences in disclosure timing, format, or information access. Second, the signal is inherently ambiguous: a firm can change auditors for
benign reasons (e.g., audit committee rotation policy) or for troubling ones (e.g.,
disagreements over accounting estimates), and investors must form their own
assessments. Third, auditing is a regulated, institution-dependent activity, making
trust in regulatory infrastructure --- which is precisely what polarization erodes
--- central to how investors evaluate the disclosure.

Our theoretical framework builds on the heterogeneous-priors model of
\citet{kandel1995}. Investors observe a public audit signal but differ in how
they interpret it. This heterogeneity can arise through at least three
channels: differences in prior beliefs about firm quality and audit
credibility, differences in institutional trust --- particularly trust in
auditors, the PCAOB, and regulatory oversight more broadly --- and differences
in perceived signal precision, where some investors treat the disclosure as
highly diagnostic while others discount it as noisy or strategically motivated.
Political polarization amplifies all three channels. The recent political
science literature distinguishes \emph{ideological} polarization (divergence
in issue positions) from \emph{affective} or \emph{social} polarization
(partisan animus, distrust, and identity-driven bias that can intensify even
when policy views are moderate; \citealp{mason2015, druckmanlevy2021}).
Auditor disclosures are likely affected by both: ideological polarization may
drive divergent substantive interpretations of the same event, while affective
polarization may drive divergent assessments of whether auditors and
regulators are credible. Under standard models of belief-driven trading
\citep{harris1993}, greater posterior dispersion translates into more trading
volume and larger absolute price movements. The mechanism is distinct from
information asymmetry: polarization does not change what is disclosed, only
how it is processed.

Empirically, we measure the political polarization of the firm's surrounding
environment at the congressional district level using the \citet{esteban1994}
polarization index applied to U.S. House election returns from 2000 to 2024.
Because investor-level political identity is not directly observable, the
headquarters-state political environment serves as a reduced-form proxy for
the polarization of the investor and stakeholder context in which the
disclosure is received. Our primary specifications test the ideological-
polarization channel directly; we treat the current empirical design as
an initial test of the broader mechanism, with affective polarization
examined as a supplementary validation rather than a separately identified
channel. Our main specification regresses abnormal trading volume and absolute
cumulative abnormal returns (CARs) over the $[-1, +1]$ window around auditor
change disclosures on the state-level polarization measure at the time of the
event, controlling for firm characteristics and including year and industry
fixed effects.

This paper makes three contributions. First, we contribute to the auditing and
capital markets literature by documenting that the market response to audit signals
depends on the political environment in which investors are embedded. Prior work
establishes that audit reports and auditor-related events convey information to
markets \citep{firth1978, dodd1984, defond2014}; we show that the same disclosure
generates more extreme reactions in more politically polarized surrounding
environments. Second, we contribute to the growing literature on political economy and
capital markets \citep{kempf2024}. Existing work focuses primarily on asset
allocation, partisan trading behavior, and analyst forecasts; we show that
political polarization also shapes how investors interpret mandatory audit
disclosures, a setting where institutional trust plays an unusually central
role. Third,
we contribute to the theoretical literature on heterogeneous beliefs and trading
\citep{harris1993, kandel1995, hong2007} by providing an empirical setting where
the source of belief heterogeneity --- political polarization --- is plausibly
exogenous to firm-level reporting decisions and varies in well-defined ways over
time.

Our findings have implications for audit standard-setters and regulators. A common
motivation for expanded audit reporting requirements (e.g., critical audit matters
under PCAOB AS 3101) is that richer disclosures will reduce investor uncertainty
and improve market efficiency. Our results suggest a limit to this logic: when
investors differ in their priors about institutional credibility, more information
does not eliminate disagreement --- it may instead amplify it. The findings suggest one limit to disclosure-based regulation: when
investors differ in their assessments of institutional credibility, additional
disclosure may amplify rather than resolve disagreement. Although we study
auditor changes as an initial tractable setting, the mechanism may operate
wherever disclosure interpretation depends on trust in regulated gatekeepers ---
including critical audit matters,
going-concern opinions, and internal control reports.

% ── 2. Related Literature ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Related Literature}
\label{sec:lit}

\subsection{Audit Reports and Capital Markets}

A substantial literature documents that auditor-related disclosures convey
information to investors. Early studies show that qualified audit opinions affect
stock prices \citep{firth1978, dodd1984}. Subsequent work examines how audit
quality relates to market outcomes more broadly \citep{francis2004, defond2014}.
Research on expanded audit reporting --- including critical audit matters under
PCAOB AS 3101 --- finds mixed evidence on incremental informativeness, with some
studies finding that richer disclosures affect investor decisions
\citep{christensen2014} and others finding limited price effects
\citep{gutierrez2018}. We build on this literature by asking not whether audit
signals are informative in the aggregate, but whether their interpretation varies
systematically with the political environment.

\subsection{Auditor Changes}

Auditor changes are among the most salient audit-related events. They are
mandatory disclosures (Item 4.01 of Form 8-K), filed within four business days,
and must include information about the reason for the change and any disagreements.
Prior work shows that markets react to auditor changes, with the direction and
magnitude depending on the nature of the switch \citep{johnson1990, defond1998,
landsman2009}. Big-4 departures tend to generate negative abnormal returns, while
Big-4 appointments are viewed more favorably. We use these events as our primary
setting because the mandatory, standardized disclosure format makes them ideal for
studying cross-sectional differences in interpretation.

\subsection{Political Economy of Auditing}

A related literature examines how political factors influence audit outcomes.
\citet{chan2006} document that political connections shape auditor reporting
behavior and auditor switching in a cross-country setting, showing that audit
opinions respond to political incentives rather than purely economic ones.
More broadly, the political economy of auditing encompasses how regulatory,
institutional, and political pressures shape the supply of audit quality
\citep{francis2004, defond2014}. \citet{lennox2010} show that Big~4 auditor
oversight is associated with lower fraud incidence, underscoring that the
institutional infrastructure surrounding auditing---including regulatory
design and oversight---has measurable consequences for financial reporting
quality.

Our paper examines a different and largely unexplored dimension: the
\emph{investor side} of the audit signal. Even holding the content of an
audit disclosure constant, political polarization may alter how investors
interpret it. This mechanism is particularly salient in the context of
PCAOB oversight, whose authority and credibility as a regulatory body may
not be assessed uniformly across investor groups. Investors who are
more skeptical of regulatory institutions---a tendency documented to vary
systematically with political identity \citep{kempf2024}---may discount the
credibility of auditor disclosures differently from investors who trust
regulatory oversight. The result is that the same Form~8-K filing can
generate heterogeneous investor reactions not because investors differ in
what information they possess, but because they differ in their assessment
of the signal's institutional credibility. This mechanism is orthogonal to
the auditor-side political economy literature and complements it: even if
political pressures leave auditor behavior unchanged, polarization in the
investor population can amplify the capital-market consequences of those
disclosures.

\subsection{Political Polarization and Capital Markets}

A rapidly growing literature documents that political polarization affects
capital markets. \citet{kempf2024} survey evidence that partisan identity
influences asset allocation, trading behavior, and return expectations. Related
work finds that political beliefs shape economic forecasts and risk perceptions.
A key distinction in the broader political science literature is between
\emph{ideological} polarization --- divergence in substantive issue positions
--- and \emph{affective} or \emph{social} polarization --- partisan animus,
distrust, and identity-driven bias \citep{druckmanlevy2021}. \citet{mason2015}
shows that partisan sorting can intensify bias and anger even without
commensurate growth in issue extremity, suggesting that identity alignment
alone can drive divergent responses to the same information. This distinction
is important for our setting: auditor disclosures are institution-dependent
signals whose interpretation may be shaped by both substantive policy
disagreement and affective distrust of gatekeepers. We extend this literature
to the auditing context, where institutional trust plays an unusually central
role.

\subsection{Heterogeneous Beliefs and Trading}

Our theoretical framework draws on models of heterogeneous beliefs in financial
markets. \citet{harris1993} show that differences of opinion generate trade even
without private information. \citet{kandel1995} demonstrate that investors with
heterogeneous priors interpret identical public signals differently, generating
trading volume and price effects. \citet{hong2007} survey the broader literature
on disagreement and asset prices. We contribute to this literature by linking
belief heterogeneity to a specific, measurable source --- political polarization
--- and by identifying the audit disclosure setting as a clean empirical context.

% ── 3. Theory ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Theory}
\label{sec:theory}

We formalize polarization as a latent force that increases heterogeneity in
investors' interpretation of auditor-originated disclosures. The Bayesian model
that follows operationalizes this heterogeneity through prior beliefs, which is
the most tractable formal channel. Institutional trust and perceived signal
precision should be understood as reduced-form sources of prior-like
heterogeneity: a less trusting investor effectively places a lower prior on the
informativeness of the auditor's signal; an investor who perceives lower signal
precision effectively flattens the likelihood function. Either way, the
formal result is the same increase in posterior dispersion. The model therefore
provides a microfoundation for the broader mechanism described in the
introduction, not a claim that priors are the only operative channel.
The model draws on the heterogeneous-priors framework of \citet{kandel1995}.

\subsection{Setting}

Consider a firm whose true quality $\theta \in \{0, 1\}$ is unknown to
investors. The firm discloses an auditor-related signal $s \in \{0, 1\}$ (e.g.,
an auditor change announcement). Signals are informative: $P(s=1 \mid
\theta=1) = p_H > P(s=1 \mid \theta=0) = p_L$, with $p_H > p_L$.

A continuum of investors $i \in [0,1]$ observe $s$ and form posterior beliefs.
Investors share the same likelihood function $\{p_H, p_L\}$ but differ in their
prior beliefs $\pi_i \equiv P_i(\theta = 1) \in (0,1)$.

\textbf{Source of prior heterogeneity.} We model investors as belonging to
ideological groups indexed by political affiliation $g \in \{D, R\}$, with
population shares $s_D$ and $s_R = 1 - s_D$. Group-level priors reflect
differential trust in auditors and regulatory institutions: $\pi_D \neq \pi_R$.
In the model, all heterogeneity is captured by differences in $\pi_i$; in
practice, polarization may also affect how investors perceive signal precision
($p_H, p_L$) or how much weight they place on the disclosure relative to their
priors. The reduced-form predictions are the same: greater political distance
generates greater dispersion in posterior beliefs.
Polarization increases the \emph{dispersion} of priors across groups without
changing the \emph{average} prior.

\subsection{Belief Updating}

Investor $i$'s posterior belief after observing $s=1$ is:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:bayes}
    P_i(\theta = 1 \mid s = 1)
    = \frac{p_H \, \pi_i}{p_H \, \pi_i + p_L (1 - \pi_i)}
    \equiv \phi(\pi_i).
\end{equation}
The function $\phi(\cdot)$ is strictly increasing and concave in $\pi_i$.

We now establish the central comparative static of the model: that any
widening of the gap between group priors --- holding the average prior
fixed --- increases the dispersion of posterior beliefs. This is the
formal sense in which polarization amplifies disagreement.

\begin{proposition}[Polarization and belief dispersion]
    \label{prop:dispersion}
    Fix population shares $s_D, s_R > 0$ with $s_D + s_R = 1$ and mean prior
    $\bar{\pi} = s_D \pi_D + s_R \pi_R$. The variance of posterior beliefs
    across investor groups is
    \[
        \mathrm{Var}_i[\phi(\pi_i)]
        = s_D s_R \bigl(\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R)\bigr)^2.
    \]
    For any increase in $|\pi_D - \pi_R|$ that holds $\bar{\pi}$ fixed,
    $\mathrm{Var}_i[\phi(\pi_i)]$ strictly increases.
\end{proposition}

\noindent\textit{Proof.} See Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}. $\square$

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Remark.} Proposition~\ref{prop:dispersion} isolates the
mechanism through which polarization enters the model. The key condition is
a \emph{mean-preserving spread} of priors: polarization shifts the two groups
farther apart while leaving the population-average prior $\bar\pi$ unchanged.
This rules out the alternative that polarization merely correlates with
aggregate pessimism or optimism about firm quality. Under this condition, the
posterior variance increases strictly --- more polarized priors generate more
dispersed beliefs after observing the same signal. The proposition thereby
provides the microfoundation for both empirical predictions: more dispersed
posteriors produce higher disagreement-driven trading volume (Prediction~1)
and, through demand imbalance, larger absolute price movements (Prediction~2).
The result is stated for a two-group setting for tractability; the same
monotonicity extends to richer prior distributions under mean-preserving
spreads, by the convexity of Bayesian updating when likelihoods are
well-separated.

\subsection{Equilibrium Prices and Trading}

Following \citet{harris1993}, prices aggregate beliefs:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:price}
    P^* = \int_0^1 w_i \, \phi(\pi_i) \, di,
\end{equation}
where $w_i$ are equilibrium portfolio weights. Trading volume is increasing in
posterior belief dispersion \citep{kandel1995}:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:volume}
    V \text{ increasing in } \mathrm{Var}_i[\phi(\pi_i)].
\end{equation}

The equilibrium price $P^*$ is a weighted average of investors' posterior
beliefs. By Proposition~\ref{prop:dispersion}, an increase in polarization
that constitutes a mean-preserving spread of priors leaves $P^*$ unchanged
in expectation but increases the cross-sectional dispersion of posteriors.
This has two distinct empirical implications that motivate our outcome
variables.

First, when some investors revise beliefs strongly upward and others strongly
downward in response to the same signal, the absolute price movement
$|P^* - P_0|$ is increasing in posterior dispersion even when the average
direction is zero. This maps directly to our first empirical outcome, the
absolute cumulative abnormal return $|CAR[-1,+1]|$: a more polarized
investor base produces larger absolute price reactions to the same
auditor-change disclosure without necessarily producing a predictable
direction. In this sense the model predicts cross-sectional variation in
\emph{how much} prices move, not \emph{which way} they move.

Second, trade is generated by the meeting of investors who update positively
with those who update negatively. Equation~\eqref{eq:volume} formalizes the
\citet{kandel1995} result that disagreement-driven turnover is proportional
to the variance of posterior beliefs: more dispersed posteriors mean more
investor pairs with mutually beneficial trades, and hence higher aggregate
volume. This maps to our second outcome variable, abnormal trading volume
($AbVol$), constructed as event-window share turnover scaled by a pre-event
benchmark. Combining equations \eqref{eq:price} and \eqref{eq:volume} with
Proposition~\ref{prop:dispersion}, the model predicts that both $|CAR|$ and
$AbVol$ are increasing in political polarization. The prediction for volume
is especially clean: unlike price, which depends on equilibrium weights
$w_i$ that can in principle offset belief dispersion, volume rises
monotonically with the variance of posteriors under standard no-short-selling
or symmetric-trading assumptions \citep{harris1993}.

\subsection{Role of Signal Ambiguity}
\label{sec:ambiguity}

The Bayesian framework also generates a comparative static with respect to
signal informativeness that is central to our empirical design. Differentiating
the posterior with respect to the prior:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:sensitivity}
    \phi'(\pi_i) = \frac{p_H \, p_L}{\bigl[p_H \pi_i + p_L(1-\pi_i)\bigr]^2}.
\end{equation}
This sensitivity is \emph{decreasing} in $p_H - p_L$: a highly informative
signal ($p_H - p_L$ large) dominates the prior, so that all investors converge
toward the same posterior regardless of their starting point, and heterogeneous
priors contribute little to posterior dispersion. In the extreme case of a fully
revealing signal ($p_H = 1, p_L = 0$), every investor---regardless of prior---
updates to the same posterior, and polarization has no effect. Conversely, when
$p_H - p_L$ is small---when the signal is difficult to map to a clear valuation
implication---posteriors remain close to priors, and prior heterogeneity
translates more directly into posterior dispersion. It follows that the
marginal effect of polarization on disagreement is larger for more ambiguous
signals.

In our setting, signal ambiguity varies systematically across auditor-change
events. An auditor departure following a disclosed disagreement over accounting
estimates or a qualified opinion is more informationally salient---and therefore
less ambiguous---than a routine rotation. A switch from a non-Big-4 to a Big-4
auditor carries a clear quality connotation; a Big-4-to-Big-4 lateral move is
harder to evaluate. Events with no stated reason for the change, or where the
disclosed rationale is generic (e.g., ``to reduce costs''), leave investors
with greater interpretive latitude. We construct a \texttt{high\_ambiguity}
indicator from observable filing characteristics and interact it with the
polarization measure in our main specifications (Section~\ref{sec:design}).
The ambiguity amplification prediction is a testable restriction of the model:
it requires not just a polarization main effect but a
polarization\,$\times$\,ambiguity interaction with positive sign.

\subsection{Testable Predictions}

\begin{itemize}
    \item[\textbf{P1.}] \textit{Polarization and trading volume (primary formal
    prediction).} Equation~\eqref{eq:volume} and the \citet{kandel1995}
    mechanism predict that disagreement-driven turnover is increasing in
    posterior belief variance. Greater polarization generates more investor
    pairs with divergent posteriors and hence mutually beneficial trading
    opportunities, raising aggregate volume even in the absence of private
    information. This prediction follows directly from
    Proposition~\ref{prop:dispersion}: polarization increases posterior
    variance, which increases $V$. We test P1 using abnormal turnover
    ($AbVol$) as the outcome, constructed as event-window share turnover
    scaled by a pre-event benchmark.

    \item[\textbf{P2.}] \textit{Polarization and absolute returns (secondary
    empirical prediction).} When investors on opposing sides of the prior
    distribution revise beliefs in opposite directions, absolute price
    movements are amplified even when the average signed reaction is close to
    zero. This prediction is consistent with the model's dispersion mechanism,
    but the link from posterior variance to $|CAR|$ depends additionally on
    demand asymmetry, short-sale constraints, or limits to arbitrage that are
    not explicitly modeled here \citep{hong2007}. We therefore treat $|CAR|$
    as a complementary empirical outcome: evidence that polarization increases
    both $AbVol$ and $|CAR|$ is more informative than either alone, but $AbVol$
    is the cleaner formal prediction. We test P2 by regressing $|CAR[-1,+1]|$
    on the state-year polarization measure, with the same controls and fixed
    effects as P1.

    \item[\textbf{P3.}] \textit{Ambiguity amplification.} The signal-sensitivity
    result (Section~\ref{sec:ambiguity}) predicts that polarization effects
    are larger when audit signals are more ambiguous---that is, when $p_H -
    p_L$ is smaller. We operationalize this as an interaction between the
    polarization measure and a \texttt{high\_ambiguity} indicator constructed
    from observable filing characteristics. The prediction requires a positive
    and significant interaction coefficient, above and beyond any polarization
    main effect. This test is important because it distinguishes the
    \emph{interpretation channel} from a simpler \emph{salience} story: if
    polarization merely amplified attention to any salient news, the
    polarization effect would not be systematically larger for inherently
    ambiguous events.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Discussion.} The model formalizes one specific channel---heterogeneous
priors---but the empirical predictions extend to the broader set of mechanisms
described in Section~\ref{sec:theory}. If polarization instead operates
through institutional trust, investors in more polarized environments differ
more sharply in the credibility they assign to the disclosure, producing the
same increase in posterior dispersion. If polarization operates through
perceived signal precision, some investors treat the 8-K as highly diagnostic
while others discount it as strategically motivated, again widening beliefs.
In each case, the core prediction is the same: polarization amplifies
disagreement-sensitive market outcomes. The ambiguity interaction in P3 is
robust to all three channels---ambiguous signals leave more room for
institutional trust and prior heterogeneity to drive divergent updating.
A further implication is that standard disclosure-based regulatory remedies
cannot fully eliminate polarization-driven disagreement, because the
heterogeneity lies in how investors process signals, not in what the signals
contain.

% ── 4. Hypothesis Development ─────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Hypothesis Development}
\label{sec:hypotheses}

\begin{hyp}
    \label{h1}
    Investor reactions to auditor change disclosures are more extreme in
    politically polarized environments. Specifically, both the absolute
    cumulative abnormal return ($|CAR|$) and abnormal trading volume around
    auditor change events are increasing in political polarization.
\end{hyp}

\begin{hyp}
    \label{h2}
    The polarization effect on market reactions is stronger for higher-stakes
    auditor changes --- specifically, auditor dismissals. Dismissals are
    initiated by the client, often amid concerns about financial reporting or
    accounting judgments, making them more informationally consequential than
    routine resignations. Higher stakes amplify whatever interpretation investors
    form: more is riding on the outcome, so polarization-induced divergence in
    interpretation translates into larger disagreement and more extreme reactions.
    This is a salience and stakes prediction, not an ambiguity prediction ---
    dismissals need not be more ambiguous than resignations to generate stronger
    polarization effects; they need only be more consequential.
\end{hyp}

\begin{hyp}
    \label{h3}
    The polarization effect on abnormal trading volume and absolute returns is
    stronger for auditor changes that carry more ambiguous signals --- events
    where the informativeness of the disclosure ($p_H - p_L$) is lower.
    Per Section~\ref{sec:ambiguity}, ambiguity amplifies the role of prior
    heterogeneity in determining posterior dispersion. This is a separate and
    complementary prediction to H2: H2 concerns the stakes of the event; H3
    concerns how much interpretive latitude the event leaves investors.
\end{hyp}

% ── 5. Empirical Design ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Empirical Design}
\label{sec:design}

\subsection{Sample Construction}

\textbf{Auditor change events.} We identify auditor changes using Form 8-K
filings disclosing a change in the registrant's certifying accountant (Item
4.01), obtained from SEC EDGAR for 2001--2024.\footnote{We end the sample in
2024 because DW-NOMINATE ideological scores for the 119th Congress (which
began January 2025) are not yet available in final form from Voteview. The
Esteban-Ray polarization measure is unaffected, as the 2024 U.S. House
election results are available from the MIT Election Lab.} We retain only
original 8-K filings matched to a CRSP PERMNO and require at least 100
non-missing trading days in the estimation window. The final sample consists
of [N] auditor change events for [N] unique firms. We exclude financial firms
(SIC 6000--6999) and utilities (SIC 4900--4999).

\subsection{Dependent Variables}

\textbf{Absolute cumulative abnormal return ($|CAR|$).} Following standard
event-study methodology \citep{mackinlay1997}, we estimate a market model
over the estimation window $[-252, -46]$ trading days relative to the event
date, using at least 100 non-missing trading days to estimate parameters:
\begin{equation}
    AR_{i,t} = R_{i,t} - (\hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\beta}_i \cdot R_{m,t}),
    \quad t \in \{-1, 0, +1\}.
\end{equation}
The window begins 252 days before the event to capture a full trading year
of pre-event returns and ends 46 days before to avoid contamination from
information leakage in the run-up period. $CAR_i = \sum_{t=-1}^{+1} AR_{i,t}$
and $|CAR_i|$ is the primary dependent variable. We use the CRSP
value-weighted return as the market return and include delisting returns
following \citet{beaver2007}.

\textbf{Abnormal trading volume ($AbVol$).} We measure trading-volume
reactions over the $[-1, +1]$ event window, consistent with the short-run
agreement-to-disagree framework of \citet{kandel1995} in which volume spikes
at the moment beliefs diverge. $AbVol$ is the mean of
$[\log(\mathrm{Vol}_{i,t}+1) - \overline{\log(\mathrm{Vol}_i)}]$ over the
$[-1,+1]$ window, where the baseline is the estimation window mean.

\subsection{Political Polarization Measure}

A central empirical challenge is that the theory's polarization object ---
the latent force that increases divergence in interpretation through identity,
alienation, and perceived institutional credibility --- is not directly
observable at the investor level \citep{duclos2004}. The available data
provide only grouped or indirect proxies, each of which captures a different
margin of the latent concept \citep{esteban2007}. We therefore use three
complementary proxies rather than treating any single measure as definitive.
The ER election-return measure below captures \emph{electoral sorting and
local political division}; DW-NOMINATE captures \emph{elite ideological
distance}; and the ANES affective series captures \emph{national partisan
animus}. These are not three ways of measuring the same thing; they
triangulate a latent object from distinct angles \citep{wang2000}.\footnote{%
The continuous-density estimators developed by \citet{duclos2004} provide
the most rigorous formalization of the identity--alienation concept, but
require a directly observed distribution of the underlying investor-level
trait. Because our setting does not yield a firm-year density of investor
political identity, we use reduced-form political-environment proxies instead.}

We apply the \citet{esteban1994} polarization index to U.S. House election
returns. For congressional district $d$ in election year $e$:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:er}
    P^{ER}_{d,e} = s_{D,d,e} \cdot s_{R,d,e}
    \cdot \left(s_{D,d,e}^\alpha + s_{R,d,e}^\alpha\right), \quad \alpha = 1,
\end{equation}
which simplifies to $s_{D} \cdot s_{R}$ when $\alpha=1$, maximized at 0.25 when
the two parties split the vote equally. We aggregate to the state level weighting
by each district's share of total state two-party votes, forward-fill to annual
frequency --- assigning each election year's measure to the two calendar years
until the next election, on the assumption that the surrounding political
environment evolves at the electoral-cycle frequency rather than annually ---
and standardize to mean zero and unit variance.\footnote{Mehlhaff
(2023) shows that, at $\alpha=1$, the Esteban-Ray index is nearly
indistinguishable from the variance of the two-party vote distribution
(cross-sample correlation $\approx 0.93$), and argues that standard polarization
indices conflate inter-group heterogeneity with intra-group homogeneity. We
acknowledge this limitation. Because our primary interest is the polarization
\emph{mechanism} rather than the specific index, our main robustness tests
exploit DW-NOMINATE ideological scores and ANES affective polarization
(Section~\ref{sec:design}), which are constructed from entirely different data
sources and do not share this limitation. Convergent evidence across all three
measures would indicate that the findings are not an artifact of the
Esteban-Ray parameterization.}

Because different polarization indices emphasize different aspects of
between-group separation and within-group cohesion, the baseline electoral
measure cannot be treated as exhaustive \citep{wang2000}. In the terminology
of \citet{esteban2007}, any grouped empirical proxy is a representation of
a richer latent object that abstracts from within-group dispersion. The ER
district-level proxy captures electoral division and local sorting but does
not separately identify within-group ideological cohesion. A complementary
measure is therefore needed.

As a second proxy for polarization, we construct a measure of elite ideological
distance from DW-NOMINATE first-dimension scores \citep{poole1997,poole2007}.
DW-NOMINATE scores capture legislators' revealed ideological positions from
roll-call voting records, rather than electoral outcomes, and thus measure
\emph{ideological} polarization directly. For each state $s$ and Congress $c$,
we compute:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:dw}
    P^{DW}_{s,c} = \overline{\text{dim1}}_{R,s,c}
    - \overline{\text{dim1}}_{D,s,c},
\end{equation}
where $\overline{\text{dim1}}_{R,s,c}$ and $\overline{\text{dim1}}_{D,s,c}$
are the mean first-dimension DW-NOMINATE scores among House Republicans and
Democrats from state $s$ in Congress $c$, respectively. Because the first
dimension captures the liberal--conservative continuum, $P^{DW}_{s,c}$ is
always positive and increasing in ideological distance between the parties.
We expand each Congress's scores to the two calendar years it covers and
standardize to mean zero and unit variance. The national-level counterpart,
$P^{DW\text{-nat}}_{c}$, computes the same gap across all House members
nationally and varies only over time, providing a pure time-series check.
The ER and DW-NOMINATE measures are alternative proxies: the ER measure
captures electoral competitiveness, while DW-NOMINATE captures ideological
distance. If both predict the same investor response, the evidence for a
polarization mechanism is substantially stronger.

\subsection{Supplementary Affective Polarization Measure}

The identity--alienation framework of \citet{duclos2004} suggests that
issue distance is only one component of polarization. Affective antagonism
--- partisan animus, out-group distrust, and social distance --- is
conceptually distinct from ideological divergence and is especially relevant
for a mechanism operating through institutional trust. Both the ER and
DW-NOMINATE measures primarily capture ideological polarization; neither
directly measures how much partisans distrust each other or the institutions
the opposing side tends to support. We therefore supplement these with a
national affective polarization series. This series does not replace the
ideological proxies but tests whether the paper's finding is also present
when polarization is measured through partisan animus \citep{druckmanlevy2021,
mason2015}.

We construct the affective polarization series from the ANES Time Series
Cumulative Data File. In each survey year with available data (2000, 2004,
2008, 2012, 2016, 2020, 2024), we compute:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:ap}
    AP_t = \frac{1}{2}\left[
        \overline{\Delta_D}_t + \overline{\Delta_R}_t
    \right],
\end{equation}
where $\overline{\Delta_D}_t = \mathbb{E}_t[\text{Dem therm} - \text{Rep
therm} \mid \text{Dem partisan}]$ and $\overline{\Delta_R}_t =
\mathbb{E}_t[\text{Rep therm} - \text{Dem therm} \mid \text{Rep partisan}]$
are the mean in-party minus out-party feeling thermometer differentials among
partisan identifiers \citep{iyengar2012}. We linearly interpolate between
survey years to produce an annual series.

Because $AP_t$ is national, it is absorbed by year fixed effects when entered
alone. We therefore identify its role through interaction with a
time-invariant state-level \emph{exposure} variable: the long-run average
absolute Republican presidential vote margin,
$\text{Exposure}_s = \overline{|R\text{-share}_{s,t} - 0.5|}$, averaged
over all presidential elections from 2000 to 2024. High-exposure states
(e.g., Wyoming, Massachusetts) consistently vote lopsidedly; low-exposure
states (e.g., Pennsylvania, Wisconsin) are genuine swing states. The
identifying variation is thus $AP_t \times \text{Exposure}_s$: the
differential response of firms in high-partisan-intensity states as national
affective polarization rises over time. We present this as a supplementary
validation in Table~\ref{tab:affective}, not as a coequal baseline
specification, because the national AP series cannot be assigned to states
without the exposure interaction.

\subsection{Empirical Specification}

The unit of observation is an auditor change event $e$. We estimate:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:main}
    Y_e = \alpha + \beta\,\textit{Pol}_{s(e),\,t(e)}
    + \gamma' X_{e,\,t(e)-1}
    + \tau_{t(e)} + \delta_{j(e)} + \varepsilon_e,
\end{equation}
where $Y_e$ is $|CAR_e|$ or $AbVol_e$; $\textit{Pol}_{s(e),t(e)}$ is the
standardized state political polarization measure for the headquarters state
$s(e)$ of the event's firm in event year $t(e)$ --- a reduced-form proxy
for the polarization of the political environment in which the disclosure is
received, not a direct measure of investor political identity;
$X_{e,t(e)-1}$ is a vector of firm controls measured in the fiscal year prior
to the event (size, leverage, ROA, book-to-market, loss indicator, and sales
growth); $\tau_{t(e)}$ are event-year fixed effects; and $\delta_{j(e)}$ are
2-digit SIC industry fixed effects. Standard errors are clustered at the firm
level. The coefficient of interest is $\beta$.

\subsection{Identification}

The paper identifies whether auditor-change disclosures are interpreted
differently in more polarized political environments --- not whether any
specific type of investor trades more. Because investor-level political
identity is unobserved, the empirical design uses the headquarters-state
political environment as a reduced-form proxy for the polarization of the
context in which the disclosure is received.

Identification rests on two sources of variation in $\textit{Pol}_{s,e}$:
cross-state differences in the partisan environment of congressional elections,
and time-series changes in that environment across election cycles. Year
fixed effects absorb aggregate macroeconomic and political conditions common
to all firms. Industry fixed effects absorb sector-level differences in
auditor change frequency and investor base composition. The key identifying
assumption is that, conditional on these controls, a firm's headquarters-state
polarization at the time of the event is uncorrelated with unobserved
determinants of the market's reaction to that specific auditor change.

The main threat is that politically polarized states may attract different
investor clienteles or have systematically different firm characteristics that
correlate with event-window returns. We address this through rich firm-level
controls, and obtain a stronger within-state interpretation in specifications
that add state fixed effects (Table~\ref{tab:robust}, column 6): these
absorb all time-invariant cross-state differences and identify solely from
changes in polarization within the same state across election cycles.
Because the polarization regressor varies at the state$\times$year level,
column (7) of Table~\ref{tab:robust} reports state-level clustering as a
robustness check; the baseline clusters at the firm level, which is
conservative for event-level inference but optimistic relative to the
regressor's level of variation.\footnote{A finer-grained alternative would
match firms to county-level polarization using the MIT Election Lab
county-level presidential returns and the firm's headquarter county from
Compustat. County-level matching provides sharper geographic identification
and avoids averaging across heterogeneous within-state counties.
The cost is that the ER index requires repeated competitive elections; county
presidential returns are available only quadrennially, and congressional
district boundaries do not align with county boundaries. We treat county-level
matching as a feasible robustness check to be reported in the online appendix.}

% ── 7. Conclusion ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Political polarization affects how investors interpret auditor signals, with
implications for market efficiency and audit regulation. Identical disclosures
generate more extreme market reactions --- larger absolute returns and higher
trading volume --- in more polarized political environments. These effects are
stronger for ambiguous signals, consistent with a model in which polarization
increases polarization-induced heterogeneity in interpretation.

The findings suggest one limit to disclosure-based regulation in
credibility-sensitive settings: when investors differ in their assessments of
institutional trustworthiness, more disclosure does not eliminate disagreement. As
polarization has increased persistently over the past two decades, its effect
on audit signal interpretation may be growing. We study auditor changes as an
initial tractable setting, but the mechanism should operate wherever disclosure
interpretation depends on trust in regulated gatekeepers. Future research
should examine whether these patterns extend to critical audit matters, going
concern opinions, and internal control reports. A further promising direction
is to distinguish the roles of ideological and affective polarization ---
our proxies primarily capture ideological distance, but the recent political
science literature suggests that partisan distrust and identity-based animus
\citep{mason2015, druckmanlevy2021} may be independently important for how
investors assess the credibility of institution-dependent disclosures.

\section{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:dispersion}}
\label{app:proofs}

\noindent\textbf{Setup.} Let there be two investor groups, $D$ and $R$, with
population shares $s_D, s_R > 0$ and $s_D + s_R = 1$. Group $g$ holds prior
$\pi_g \in (0,1)$. The mean prior is $\bar{\pi} = s_D \pi_D + s_R \pi_R$.
After observing signal $s=1$, investor $i$ in group $g$ holds posterior
$\phi(\pi_g)$, where the Bayesian updating function is
\[
    \phi(\pi) = \frac{p_H \pi}{p_H \pi + p_L(1-\pi)},
    \qquad p_H > p_L > 0.
\]
The function $\phi$ is strictly increasing and continuous on $(0,1)$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 1: Variance formula.}
The posterior beliefs across groups follow a two-point distribution placing
mass $s_D$ at $\phi(\pi_D)$ and mass $s_R$ at $\phi(\pi_R)$. The population
mean posterior is $\bar{\phi} = s_D \phi(\pi_D) + s_R \phi(\pi_R)$. A
standard calculation gives:
\begin{align*}
    \mathrm{Var}_i[\phi(\pi_i)]
    &= s_D\bigl(\phi(\pi_D) - \bar\phi\bigr)^2
     + s_R\bigl(\phi(\pi_R) - \bar\phi\bigr)^2 \\
    &= s_D (s_R)^2 \bigl(\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R)\bigr)^2
     + s_R (s_D)^2 \bigl(\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R)\bigr)^2 \\
    &= s_D s_R (s_D + s_R) \bigl(\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R)\bigr)^2 \\
    &= s_D s_R \bigl(\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R)\bigr)^2,
\end{align*}
where the second equality uses $\phi(\pi_D) - \bar\phi = s_R(\phi(\pi_D) -
\phi(\pi_R))$ and $\phi(\pi_R) - \bar\phi = -s_D(\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R))$,
and the last equality uses $s_D + s_R = 1$. This establishes the variance
formula in Proposition~\ref{prop:dispersion}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 2: Monotonicity in $|\pi_D - \pi_R|$.}
Without loss of generality, assume $\pi_D > \pi_R$. A mean-preserving
spread that increases $|\pi_D - \pi_R|$ by $\epsilon > 0$ while holding
$\bar\pi$ fixed requires:
\[
    \pi_D' = \pi_D + s_R \epsilon, \qquad
    \pi_R' = \pi_R - s_D \epsilon,
\]
so that $s_D \pi_D' + s_R \pi_R' = s_D \pi_D + s_R \pi_R = \bar\pi$.

Since $\phi$ is strictly increasing:
\[
    \phi(\pi_D') = \phi(\pi_D + s_R\epsilon) > \phi(\pi_D),
    \qquad
    \phi(\pi_R') = \phi(\pi_R - s_D\epsilon) < \phi(\pi_R).
\]
Therefore:
\[
    \phi(\pi_D') - \phi(\pi_R')
    > \phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R) > 0.
\]
Since $s_D s_R > 0$ is unchanged, we conclude:
\[
    \mathrm{Var}_i[\phi(\pi_i')]
    = s_D s_R \bigl(\phi(\pi_D') - \phi(\pi_R')\bigr)^2
    > s_D s_R \bigl(\phi(\pi_D) - \phi(\pi_R)\bigr)^2
    = \mathrm{Var}_i[\phi(\pi_i)]. \qquad \square
\]
