\section{Introduction}
This paper examines how political polarization affects investor interpretation of auditor-related information. We propose that polarization alters belief updating, generating disagreement and heterogeneous price responses.

\section{Theory}

Consider investors receiving a signal $s$ about firm quality $\theta$. Investors differ in priors $\pi_i$.

\begin{equation}
P_i(\theta|s) = \frac{P(s|\theta)\pi_i}{P(s)}
\end{equation}

Polarization increases dispersion in $\pi_i$, leading to:

\begin{itemize}
\item Increased trading volume
\item Higher absolute returns
\end{itemize}

Ambiguous signals reduce likelihood precision, amplifying prior-driven disagreement.

\subsection{Equilibrium Implications}

Following \citet{harris1993}, heterogeneous beliefs generate trade. Prices reflect weighted beliefs:

\begin{equation}
P = \sum_i w_i E_i[\theta|s]
\end{equation}

Greater dispersion increases volatility and disagreement metrics.

\section{Empirical Predictions}

\begin{itemize}
\item Polarization $\uparrow$ $\Rightarrow$ $|CAR| \uparrow$
\item Polarization $\uparrow$ $\Rightarrow$ Volume $\uparrow$
\item Stronger effects for ambiguous audit signals
\end{itemize}

\section{Empirical Design}

We test:
\begin{equation}
CAR = \beta_1 Event + \beta_2 Polarization + \beta_3 Event \times Polarization + FE + \epsilon
\end{equation}

\section{Conclusion}
Polarization alters interpretation, not information content, of audit signals.
