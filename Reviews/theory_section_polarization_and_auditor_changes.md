\section{Theoretical Framework}

\subsection{Overview}

This paper examines how political polarization shapes investor reactions to auditor changes. Auditor changes constitute salient information events that can signal shifts in audit quality, reporting credibility, and firm governance. While prior literature largely assumes that investors process such disclosures homogeneously, we argue that political polarization induces systematic heterogeneity in how investors interpret and respond to these signals.

We develop a framework in which polarization affects not only the level of disagreement among investors, but also the underlying process through which information is interpreted. Specifically, we argue that polarization operates through identity-driven mechanisms that alter beliefs about credibility, risk, and the meaning of auditor-related disclosures.

\subsection{Distinguishing Disagreement from Polarization}

A central conceptual distinction in our framework is between investor disagreement and investor polarization. Traditional measures such as trading volume or return volatility capture dispersion in beliefs, but they do not distinguish whether such dispersion arises from differences in information sets or from differences in interpretation.

We define disagreement as differences in beliefs arising from heterogeneous information or priors. In contrast, we define polarization as systematic divergence in interpretation driven by group identity and affective attachment. Under polarization, investors do not merely hold different beliefs; they process the same information through different cognitive and evaluative frameworks.

This distinction is critical in the context of auditor changes. While disagreement implies variation in updating based on new information, polarization implies that investors may assign fundamentally different meanings to the same disclosure.

\subsection{A Multidimensional View of Polarization}

We conceptualize polarization as a multidimensional construct consisting of three interrelated components: othering, aversion, and moralization. These dimensions jointly capture how identity shapes perception, evaluation, and decision-making.

\paragraph{Othering.} Othering reflects the perception that opposing groups are fundamentally different. In capital markets, this implies that investors may view other market participants as operating under incompatible belief systems. As a result, signals such as auditor changes may be interpreted through distinct "worldviews," leading to divergent inferences about firm quality.

\paragraph{Aversion.} Aversion captures the tendency to distrust and discount the perspectives of opposing groups. This mechanism is particularly important for market outcomes. When aversion is high, investors may actively reject interpretations associated with opposing groups, leading to stronger and more polarized trading responses. This can amplify price reactions and increase trading volume, even when underlying information is ambiguous.

\paragraph{Moralization.} Moralization reflects the extent to which beliefs are tied to core moral convictions. In financial contexts, this implies that certain investors may interpret auditor credibility and corporate governance through a normative lens. Moralization can lead to rigidity in beliefs, but does not necessarily produce extreme reactions. Instead, it may affect how investors evaluate the legitimacy of signals such as auditor dismissals or resignations.

\subsection{Auditor Changes as Ambiguous Signals}

Auditor changes are inherently ambiguous events. A switch may signal improvements in audit quality (e.g., moving to a higher-quality auditor) or deterioration (e.g., disputes over accounting practices). The interpretation depends on contextual factors such as the type of change, the reputation of the auditor, and the firm’s prior reporting history.

This ambiguity makes auditor changes particularly sensitive to polarization. When signals are noisy or open to interpretation, identity-driven mechanisms play a larger role in shaping beliefs. Investors may rely on heuristics, priors, or group-based narratives when interpreting the implications of the change.

\subsection{Polarization and Belief Formation}

We propose that polarization affects belief formation through two primary channels.

First, polarization alters the weighting of information. Investors may overweight signals consistent with their prior beliefs and underweight contradictory evidence. This selective processing leads to asymmetric updating and persistent divergence in beliefs.

Second, polarization affects perceptions of credibility. Auditor changes involve judgments about the reliability of financial reporting and the integrity of management. Under polarization, investors may differ in how they assess the credibility of auditors, management, and the disclosure itself. These differences translate into heterogeneous expectations about future cash flows and risk.

\subsection{Implications for Market Outcomes}

Our framework generates several testable implications.

\paragraph{Absolute Price Reactions.} Polarization increases the dispersion of interpretations, leading to larger absolute abnormal returns around auditor change announcements. Even if average expectations do not shift dramatically, opposing reactions among investors can produce larger price movements.

\paragraph{Trading Volume.} Polarization increases the intensity of disagreement-driven trading. As investors act on divergent interpretations, trading volume rises. This effect is expected to be strongest when signals are ambiguous and when investor groups hold strongly opposing views.

\paragraph{Cross-Sectional Heterogeneity.} The effects of polarization should vary with the nature of the auditor change. For example, switches involving high-quality auditors or contentious dismissals are likely to generate greater interpretive ambiguity, amplifying the role of polarization.

\subsection{Summary}

In summary, we argue that political polarization fundamentally alters how investors process auditor-related disclosures. By introducing identity-driven interpretation into belief formation, polarization transforms auditor changes from informational events into contested signals. This perspective provides a new lens for understanding variation in market reactions and highlights the importance of incorporating behavioral and political factors into the study of auditing and capital markets.
