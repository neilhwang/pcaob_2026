\section*{Overall Evaluation}
The manuscript examines whether cross-state political polarization influences investor interpretation of mandatory auditor-change disclosures[cite: 2, 3]. The theoretical framework, building on Kandel and Pearson (1995), elegantly motivates how heterogeneous priors lead to increased trading volume[cite: 17, 76]. However, the paper currently suffers from a disconnect between the formal theory and the primary empirical tests, questionable construct validity regarding the polarization proxy, and severe statistical power limitations in the cross-sectional tests. The following five iterations of critique provide actionable steps for improvement prior to submission.

\section*{Iteration 1: Bridging the Theory-Empirics Gap}
\textbf{Critique:} The formal model (Proposition 1) yields a sharp prediction for trading volume ($AbVol$) driven by belief dispersion[cite: 104, 114]. However, the model explicitly lacks the structure to predict absolute price movements ($|CAR|$) without additional assumptions regarding market clearing or short-sale constraints[cite: 118, 119]. Despite this, $|CAR|$ is presented as the primary dependent variable in Table 2 and Hypothesis 1[cite: 152, 267]. 
\newline
\textbf{Actionable Suggestion:} 
\begin{itemize}
    \item Relegate $|CAR|$ to a secondary, exploratory outcome, or formally introduce the necessary friction (e.g., limits to arbitrage or asymmetric demand) into the model in Section 3 to mathematically derive P2.
    \item Rewrite Hypothesis 1 to position $AbVol$ as the primary test of the dispersion mechanism[cite: 135].
\end{itemize}

\section*{Iteration 2: Construct Validity of the Main Proxy}
\textbf{Critique:} The primary empirical proxy is state-level "competitiveness" defined as $1 - |D - R|$[cite: 186]. Electoral competitiveness is not synonymous with polarization. A perfectly competitive 50/50 state might consist entirely of moderates rather than polarized extremists. Furthermore, relying on "local equity bias" [cite: 234] to link a firm's headquarters state to its marginal retail investor is a heroic assumption for modern, nationally traded equities.
\newline
\textbf{Actionable Suggestion:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item You must rigorously defend why electoral competitiveness maps to the "identity-alienation" framework[cite: 178]. Consider elevating the DW-NOMINATE ideological gap [cite: 201, 203] to co-primary status, as it measures actual ideological distance rather than just vote margins.
    \item The split by firm size (Table 5, Column 10) is currently buried in robustness[cite: 298]. Make this a central test: if local equity bias is the mechanism, the polarization effect \textit{must} be monotonically decreasing in institutional ownership and firm size.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Iteration 3: Empirical Design and Power Constraints}
\textbf{Critique:} The baseline sample of 678 events over 22 years is unusually small[cite: 170]. When splitting the sample for the critical "ambiguity" test in Table 4, the high-ambiguity group is reduced to just 120 observations[cite: 278]. Concluding that the lack of significance in this group ($p = 0.422$) [cite: 281] is a meaningful non-result is dangerous; it is highly likely a Type II error due to lack of statistical power[cite: 283].
\newline
\textbf{Actionable Suggestion:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Expand the sample. Consider relaxing the stringent filter requiring at least 100 non-missing trading days [cite: 171] or broadening the definition of the events.
    \item If $N$ cannot be increased, implement and report non-parametric permutation tests or bootstrapped standard errors to prove the baseline $p$-values (e.g., $p=0.040$) [cite: 268] are not artifacts of finite-sample bias.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Iteration 4: Mathematical Proofs and Formal Model}
\textbf{Critique:} I have carefully verified the proof for Proposition 1[cite: 319, 320]. The algebra for the two-point distribution variance is correct[cite: 321]. However, the economic assumption driving the proof—a perfectly symmetrical mean-preserving spread ($\epsilon$ shift) [cite: 322]—is fragile. If affective polarization causes asymmetric shifts in priors (e.g., one group becomes highly distrustful while the other remains unchanged), the mean $\bar{\pi}$ shifts, potentially violating the core comparative static.
\newline
\textbf{Actionable Suggestion:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Add a brief corollary or discussion in Appendix A addressing what happens to posterior dispersion if the spread is non-mean-preserving.
    \item Ensure the notation in Propositions A1-A3 is consistent; $\sigma(x)$ is defined elegantly [cite: 329], but clarify if $\tau_i$ and $\kappa_i$ [cite: 331, 337] are assumed to be drawn from the same two-point group distribution as $\pi_i$.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Iteration 5: Exposition, Redundancies, and Tone}
\textbf{Critique:} The manuscript repeats certain phrasing to the point of distraction. The phrase "interpretive latitude" is heavily overused in Sections 3 and 4[cite: 126, 127, 130, 141, 156]. Additionally, the transition between the Political Economy of Auditing (2.3) [cite: 54] and Political Polarization (2.4) [cite: 62] is disjointed.
\newline
\textbf{Actionable Suggestion:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Edit for conciseness. Replace redundant uses of "interpretive latitude" with precise terms related to the specific signal attributes (e.g., "objective informational guidance" or "signal precision").
    \item Streamline the literature review by combining 2.4 and 2.5 [cite: 62, 68] to clearly delineate how ideological distance differs empirically from affective animus.
\end{itemize}
