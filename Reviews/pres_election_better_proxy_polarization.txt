1) Vertz, Frendreis, and Gibson, “Nationalization of the Electorate in the United States” (APSR, 1987).
This is the cleanest direct statement in the older literature. They analyze presidential, Senate, gubernatorial, and congressional races and conclude that “presidential races are highly nationalized, and congressional races largely localized,” while Senate and gubernatorial races have substantial state-level components. Their bottom line is that you cannot understand nationalization from congressional races alone. This directly supports the idea that presidential elections are the cleaner arena if you want a measure driven by national partisan forces rather than local factors.

2) Carson, Sievert, and Williamson, Nationalized Politics (OUP, 2023/2024).
The book’s introduction defines nationalization as presidential vote choice informing subnational elections rather than candidate-specific factors or local forces. It also notes that earlier eras allowed “more candidate-centered congressional elections,” whereas recent elections have become more nationalized. This is exactly the theoretical reason presidential outcomes are usually preferred when the object of interest is broad partisan polarization rather than office-specific electoral behavior.

3) Moskowitz, “Local News, Information, and the Nationalization of U.S. Elections” (APSR, 2021).
Moskowitz argues that local news lets voters assess down-ballot candidates separately from their national, partisan assessment. Where voters have more local information, split-ticket voting in gubernatorial and senatorial races increases. That implies the opposite as well: absent local information, down-ballot elections get pulled toward national partisan conflict. For your purposes, this supports the claim that congressional and especially state races are contaminated by varying amounts of local information and candidate differentiation, whereas presidential vote more directly reflects national partisan alignment.

4) Abramowitz and Webster, “The Rise of Negative Partisanship and the Nationalization of U.S. Elections in the 21st Century” (Electoral Studies, 2016).
This paper shows that House, Senate, and even state and local elections have become increasingly filtered through national partisan hostility. They write that voters no longer view House, Senate, and local elections as separate arenas from presidential elections, and they document growing correlations between presidential results and down-ballot outcomes. This does not mean congressional or local elections are always useless; rather, it means that when you use them, you are partly observing presidentialized national conflict plus extra office-specific noise. That is another reason presidential vote is often the cleaner proxy.

5) de Benedictis-Kessner, Lee, Velez, and Warshaw, “How Partisan Are U.S. Local Elections?” (APSR, 2025).
This is the strongest recent evidence on local elections. Using over 50 million ballots, they find that national partisanship explains more than 80% of within-contest voting variation in partisan state and local offices, but less than 10% in local nonpartisan contests and local ballot measures. So if you are thinking about using local elections as a polarization measure, this paper suggests a major distinction: partisan local races may track national polarization reasonably well, but nonpartisan local elections are much worse proxies.
