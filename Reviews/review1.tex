\section*{Referee Report on Draft: Political Polarization and Market Reactions to Auditor Changes}

\subsection*{1. Overall Evaluation}

This paper studies how political polarization affects market reactions to auditor changes. The research question is timely, relevant to both accounting and finance, and well aligned with the Conference on Auditing and Capital Markets.

However, in its current form, the paper falls short of top-tier publication standards due to:
\begin{itemize}
\item Conceptual imprecision in the definition and measurement of polarization
\item Weak linkage between theory and empirical proxies
\item Insufficient articulation of mechanisms
\item Limited engagement with cutting-edge political science literature
\end{itemize}

The paper has strong potential, but requires substantial refinement in theory, measurement, and empirical design.

\subsection*{2. Major Contribution Gap: Measurement of Polarization}

A central weakness of the draft is treating polarization as a \textbf{unidimensional construct} (e.g., vote dispersion or geographic heterogeneity).

Recent political science research shows that this is incorrect.

\textbf{Key insight from Campos & Federico (2025):}

\begin{quote}
Affective polarization is \textit{multidimensional}, consisting of:
\begin{itemize}
\item Othering (perceived fundamental differences)
\item Aversion (dislike and avoidance)
\item Moralization (identity tied to moral beliefs)
\end{itemize}
\end{quote}



Moreover, these dimensions:
\begin{itemize}
\item Are not interchangeable
\item Predict outcomes differently
\item Have distinct behavioral implications
\end{itemize}



\textbf{Implication for your paper:}

Your current polarization proxy is:
\begin{itemize}
\item Too coarse
\item Lacks behavioral interpretation
\item Cannot distinguish disagreement from hostility
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Required revision:}

You must reconceptualize polarization as:

$$
\text{Investor Disagreement} \neq \text{Investor Polarization}
$$

and explicitly argue:
\begin{itemize}
\item Volume $\rightarrow$ disagreement
\item Polarization $\rightarrow$ \textbf{interpretive divergence driven by identity}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{3. Theoretical Upgrading (Critical for Top Journals)}

Your current theory likely states:
\begin{quote}
“Polarization leads to different interpretations of auditor changes.”
\end{quote}

This is too vague.

\textbf{Using APS framework, you can sharpen this dramatically:}

\subsubsection*{Mechanism 1: Othering}
Investors perceive opposing groups as fundamentally different.

Implication:
\begin{itemize}
\item Same audit signal interpreted through different “worldviews”
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Mechanism 2: Aversion (MOST IMPORTANT)}

APS shows:
\begin{itemize}
\item Aversion strongly predicts extreme reactions and anti-democratic behavior
\end{itemize}



Implication for your paper:
\begin{itemize}
\item Investors not only disagree — they \textbf{discount opposing interpretations}
\item Leads to larger absolute returns and trading volume
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Mechanism 3: Moralization}

APS shows:
\begin{itemize}
\item Moralization can lead to rigidity but not necessarily extremity
\end{itemize}

Implication:
\begin{itemize}
\item Some investors treat auditor credibility as a moral/ideological issue
\end{itemize}

\textbf{New theoretical framing:}

\begin{quote}
Polarization affects not only beliefs but the \textit{processing of information credibility}.
\end{quote}

This is a major upgrade.

\subsection*{4. Reframing Your Contribution (Essential)}

Currently your contribution likely reads as:

\begin{quote}
“We show polarization affects market reactions.”
\end{quote}

This is not sufficient for top journals.

\textbf{Reframe as:}

\begin{quote}
This paper shows that political polarization alters the \textit{informational efficiency of audit-related disclosures} by inducing heterogeneous belief formation rooted in identity-driven interpretation.
\end{quote}

This moves your paper from:
\begin{itemize}
\item “empirical correlation”
\item to “information environment + belief formation”
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{5. Empirical Design Improvements}

\subsubsection*{5.1 Outcome Variables}

Your current choices are appropriate but need stronger interpretation:

\begin{itemize}
\item $|CAR|$ $\rightarrow$ belief dispersion
\item Volume $\rightarrow$ disagreement intensity
\end{itemize}

Tie explicitly to APS insight:
\begin{quote}
Different dimensions of polarization produce different behavioral responses
\end{quote}



\subsubsection*{5.2 Heterogeneity Tests (CRUCIAL)}

You must add:

\begin{itemize}
\item Big 4 vs non-Big 4 switches
\item Dismissal vs resignation
\item Firms with high vs low institutional ownership dispersion
\end{itemize}

These map to:
\begin{itemize}
\item signal ambiguity
\item credibility uncertainty
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{5.3 Mechanism Test (High Impact)}

If possible:

\begin{itemize}
\item Use 13F to proxy investor heterogeneity
\item Construct polarization of investor base
\end{itemize}

This directly aligns with:
\begin{quote}
polarization operates through group identity differences
\end{quote}



\subsection*{6. Identification Concerns}

Your identification is currently weak if based purely on cross-sectional variation.

Strengthen with:

\begin{itemize}
\item Event sharpness (8-K Item 4.01)
\item Narrow windows [-1, +1]
\item Firm fixed effects (if multiple events)
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{7. Positioning for Conference on Auditing and Capital Markets}

To align with the conference:

\textbf{Emphasize:}

\begin{itemize}
\item Auditor changes as credibility shocks
\item Market interpretation of audit signals
\item Information processing under polarization
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Suggested positioning sentence:}

\begin{quote}
We examine how political polarization shapes investor interpretation of auditor-related disclosures, a central mechanism through which audit quality is priced in capital markets.
\end{quote}

\subsection*{8. Writing Improvements}

\begin{itemize}
\item Replace “polarization affects reactions” with mechanism-based language
\item Avoid vague terms like “uncertainty” — specify \textbf{belief dispersion}
\item Clearly separate:
\begin{itemize}
\item disagreement
\item polarization
\end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{9. Summary of Required Revisions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Rebuild theory using multidimensional polarization (APS framework)
\item Clarify mechanism: identity-driven interpretation of audit signals
\item Strengthen empirical design with heterogeneity and mechanism tests
\item Improve positioning toward information environment literature
\item Sharpen contribution to audit and capital markets literature
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{10. Final Recommendation}

\textbf{Recommendation: Major Revision}

The idea is strong and publishable, but requires:
\begin{itemize}
\item deeper theoretical grounding
\item tighter empirical interpretation
\item stronger positioning
\end{itemize}

If these revisions are implemented, the paper has credible potential for:
\begin{itemize}
\item Conference acceptance (high probability)
\item Top accounting journal R&R (moderate probability)
\end{itemize}
